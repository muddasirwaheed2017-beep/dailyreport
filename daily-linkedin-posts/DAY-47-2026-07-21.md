## LONG-FORM POST (Personal / Founder)

**"We installed surge protection on the whole factory. Still lost three VFDs last monsoon."**

I've heard this from plant managers in Sialkot, Lahore, and Rawalpindi. Every time, the investigation finds the same thing: both SPDs were installed in the same panel board, 50 cm apart. No line inductor. No coordination.

Here's what actually happened during that monsoon strike:

1. Lightning hit the WAPDA overhead line 200 metres from the factory.
2. The surge wave (10/350 µs — the nasty, high-energy lightning waveform) travelled the line at ~100 metres per microsecond.
3. It reached the meter. The Type 1 SPD began absorbing energy.
4. 5 microseconds later, the wave front — still carrying enormous energy — reached the Type 2 SPD 50 cm away in the same panel.
5. The Type 2 (rated for the gentler 8/20 µs switching surge waveform) absorbed a surge it was never designed to handle.
6. The Type 2 failed permanently. Red indicator.
7. Nobody checked the indicator.
8. The next three storms of the season found a building with a Type 1 SPD at the meter and nothing downstream.
9. VFD 1, VFD 2, VFD 3. Each PKR 120,000–250,000.

Total equipment loss: PKR 600,000+.
Total cost of the fix they needed: PKR 7,500 for a three-phase line inductor.

---

**The rule nobody teaches at site:**

IEC 60364-5-534 requires that Type 1 and Type 2 SPDs either be separated by at least 10 metres of cable — OR have a line inductor (1.5–2 µH per pole) installed between them.

That 10 metres of cable provides ~10 µH of inductance. Enough to slow the wave rise time so the Type 1 absorbs the dangerous portion before it reaches the Type 2. The Type 2 then sees only a gentler residual surge — what it was designed to handle.

In most Pakistan buildings, this distance is achieved naturally:
→ Type 1 at the WAPDA meter at the boundary wall
→ Type 2 at the MDB inside the building
→ Cable run = 15–30 metres ✅ No inductor needed.

But in compact commercial units — shop + MDB in the same meter room, factory where the meter and incomer panel are adjacent — the distances are 1–3 metres. Coordination fails. Line inductor is mandatory.

---

**The Pakistan-specific risk:**

Sialkot district: 12–18 lightning strikes per km² per year. Among the highest in Asia.
KPK mountain zones: 12–20 strikes/km²/year.
Rawalpindi / Islamabad: 8–12 strikes/km²/year.

For these zones, Type 1+2 combined at the WAPDA meter is not optional. An overhead WAPDA line in Sialkot will receive a partial lightning strike multiple times per monsoon season. Every one of those events will test your coordination setup.

With correct coordination: a direct lightning event on the WAPDA line delivers less than 1.5 kV to the VFD input terminals. The VFD survives.

Without coordination: the same event delivers 4–8 kV to the VFD input. IGBT dies. PKR 120,000–250,000 replacement. 2–3 weeks production downtime.

---

**Don't forget the backup MCB:**

Every SPD needs a backup MCB in series on the live conductor. When the SPD exhausts its energy capacity and fails short-circuit, that MCB clears the fault before the wiring overheats.

Most Pakistan panels I've seen have no backup MCB on the SPD at all. This is a mandatory IEC 60364-5-534 requirement — not optional.

63A Type C MCB for single-phase Type 2. 125A Type C for three-phase Type 1+2 combined.

---

**And the most important maintenance step:**

Check the indicator window. Every SPD has one. Green = serviceable. Red = failed.

May: pre-monsoon inspection.
Monthly June–September: check after every major storm.
October: post-monsoon closeout.

Zone 1/3 (Sialkot, KPK, Rawalpindi): replace Type 1+2 combined every 5 years regardless of indicator — partial capacity loss doesn't show on the indicator.
