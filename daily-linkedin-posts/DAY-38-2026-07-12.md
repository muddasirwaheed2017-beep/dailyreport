### MAIN POST (Story-led format)

**The MCCB that says "25 kA" on the box — but fails catastrophically at 15 kA.**

I've seen this in two Pakistani industrial panels in the past year.

A factory buys a budget MCCB. The salesman says "25 kA breaking capacity." The engineer specifies it for a 15 kA fault-level network. Everything looks fine on paper.

Then there's a downstream short. The MCCB clears it.

Six months later: another fault. The MCCB explodes.

Why?

The "25 kA" printed on the box is **Icu** — Ultimate Breaking Capacity. It means the breaker *can* clear a 25 kA fault **once**. After that, it's mechanically degraded.

**Ics — Service Breaking Capacity** — is the value that matters for repeated protection. On most budget units, Ics = 25–50% of Icu.

A "25 kA" MCCB with Ics = 6 kA is a **6 kA device in service.**

If your network fault level is 15 kA: that budget MCCB cleared the first fault on luck. It cannot clear the second.

**3 Pakistan-Specific MCCB Selection Rules**

1. **Always buy by Ics, not Icu.** Demand the datasheet. If the seller doesn't have a datasheet with Ics clearly stated, walk away.

2. **Pakistan temperature derating:** Standard MCCBs are rated at 40°C. Pakistan panels hit 50°C in summer. A 100A MCCB only delivers ~88A at 50°C. Select one frame step up: 100A load → 125A or 160A MCCB.

3. **Generator changeover → 4-pole MCCB required.** If your generator has its own earthing, it has an isolated neutral. Using a 3-pole MCCB on a generator with local earthing eliminates neutral isolation — a serious safety fault. Always specify 4-pole for generator incomers.
