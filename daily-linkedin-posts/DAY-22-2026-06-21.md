## Topic: Changeover Switch — The Unsung Hero of Pakistan's Load Shedding Economy

---

### Long-Form LinkedIn Post (~750 words)

**Every Pakistani business owner who has ever lost data, burned a motor, or triggered a costly shutdown during a WAPDA outage — this post is for you.**

Pakistan's grid reality in 2026: 6–18 hours of load shedding depending on your feeder, your city, and the season. The solution most people reach for is a generator. But the generator alone is not enough. The piece of equipment standing between "my generator is running" and "my appliances are actually protected and WAPDA linemen aren't being electrocuted on my behalf" is the changeover switch.

And yet, it is the most under-specified piece of electrical equipment in Pakistani installations.

Here is what I see in the field consistently:

**Mistake #1: Undersized current rating.**
A 3-bedroom house running 1 ton AC, fridge, fans, and lights draws around 14–16A at 220V. The correct changeover is 32A (14.5A × 1.25 safety factor, rounded up). But the most common unit sold in local markets is a 16A unit — which runs near thermal limit every second it is energised. Contacts degrade in 12–18 months. The solution is simple: always apply the 1.25× derating factor and round up to the next standard ampere rating.

**Mistake #2: 2-pole changeover on a 3-phase supply.**
If your premises have three-phase WAPDA supply and you install a 2-pole (single-phase) changeover, you switch only one phase plus neutral to the generator. The other two phases remain live from WAPDA. Your generator is now in parallel with WAPDA on two phases — a fault condition that can result in arc flash, equipment damage, and potential grid back-feed. Always use 4-pole for 3-phase supplies. No exceptions.

**Mistake #3: ATS with zero transfer delay when a UPS is in the system.**
If your office has a UPS and a generator, and your ATS transfers within 2 seconds of WAPDA failure, the generator's cold-start voltage transient reaches the UPS input while the battery is in float charge. Over time this degrades the UPS inverter and battery. The correct setting: 30–45 second delay. The UPS bridges the gap; the generator reaches stable output; then the ATS transfers cleanly.

**Mistake #4: No neutral bonding policy.**
Pakistan's most dangerous wiring scenario: generator neutral bonded to WAPDA neutral through the changeover. If the changeover is a make-before-break type (or wired as one), current flows on both neutral conductors simultaneously. Linemen working on the "dead" WAPDA feeder are at real electrocution risk. IEC 60947-6-1 mandates a break-before-make interlock — verify it is mechanical (not just electrical) before any changeover installation.

**What the right setup looks like:**

For a Pakistani business with a 3-phase auto-start generator:
- 4-pole IEC 60947-6-1 ATS rated at 1.25× your peak load current
- MCBs on both WAPDA input and generator input terminals
- 30-second transfer delay (adjustable)
- Voltage protector/SPD on the load side for generator transient protection
- IP54 or better enclosure for the board room environment
- Phase failure relay (PFR) downstream to protect 3-phase motors from single-phasing if a generator phase fails
