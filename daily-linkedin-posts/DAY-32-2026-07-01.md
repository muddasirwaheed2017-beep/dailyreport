**The Pakistan motor protection gap: why relays trip when they shouldn't — and don't when they should**

Every Pakistani industrial facility runs a version of the same risk: a motor protection panel that was built correctly, commissioned once, and never revisited. The thermal overload relay was set by an electrician who "usually sets it to max" — and now it provides no meaningful protection while causing nuisance trips on WAPDA dip afternoons.

After analysing hundreds of motor failure calls in Pakistan's industrial market, two patterns dominate:

**Pattern 1 — Dial set to relay maximum**
A 5 HP (3.7 kW) motor on 380V 3-phase draws approximately 8.5A at full load. Install a 7–10A bimetal thermal overload relay and set the dial to 10A "for safety." Result: the motor can run at 9.9A — 116% of rated FLC — continuously, accumulating heat in the winding, until the relay finally trips at 10A. By this point, insulation damage is already occurring. The "safe" setting was actually providing 16% overload margin with no protection.

**Correct approach:** Set FLC dial to 8.5A (actual motor FLC). If WAPDA supply in your area runs below 375V during peak hours, add 5% — set to 8.9A. Never set above 110% of motor nameplate FLC.

**Pattern 2 — Wrong trip class for Pakistani starting conditions**
Pakistani tubewells, compressors, and conveyor motors are predominantly direct-on-line (DOL) started. DOL starting current is 5–7× FLC. For a 5 HP motor, that is 43–60A for 3–8 seconds. A Class 10 relay (trips at 7.2× FLC within 10 seconds) will trip during this normal starting surge. The result: the motor "keeps tripping" and the electrician either bypasses the relay entirely or sets it to maximum.

**Correct approach:** Use Trip Class 20 as the default for all Pakistani motor applications. Class 20 allows 20 seconds at 7.2× FLC — enough for DOL starting of all standard agricultural and industrial motors without nuisance tripping.

**When to upgrade from bimetal to electronic in Pakistan:**

- Motor above 22 kW (30 HP) — bimetal accuracy degrades at high currents
- Submersible pump below 100 feet — rescue cost justifies electronic protection investment
- Any motor where phase imbalance detection is required (WAPDA phase-loss events are not uncommon)
- Motors with frequent starts / intermittent duty — electronic thermal model tracks heat accumulation across duty cycles; bimetal partially cools between starts and can underprotect

---

**The WAPDA under-voltage problem:**

WAPDA supply in Pakistan regularly runs at 360–370V in residential and semi-industrial areas during peak load hours (4pm–10pm). Motor current is inversely proportional to supply voltage. At 370V instead of 380V, a motor's FLC increases approximately 2.7%. At 360V, it increases approximately 5.6%.

This means: a correctly set overload relay will appear to trip "without reason" on peak-hour afternoons — because the motor is genuinely drawing more current due to low supply voltage, and the relay is doing its job. The fix is not to raise the relay setting — the fix is to install a voltage stabiliser or use an electronic relay with automatic temperature compensation.

Setting the relay 5–8% above nameplate FLC compensates for typical WAPDA dips without eliminating overload protection.
