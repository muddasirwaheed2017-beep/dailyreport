## Primary Post (Long-form, technical, engineer audience)

**Pakistan's solar boom has created an invisible electrical hazard — and most electricians don't know it exists.**

Here is the problem:

Solar inverters — both hybrid and grid-tie, including every popular brand installed in Pakistan's 4GW+ solar surge of 2024–2026 — generate pulsating DC leakage current during normal operation.

This DC-component leakage is invisible to Type AC RCBOs.

Type AC RCBOs (the standard, cheaper type sold in Pakistan's Urdu Bazaar, Bolton Market, and every electrical shop) can only detect AC sinusoidal leakage current. When your solar inverter develops a genuine earth fault — a fault that puts your inverter chassis at mains potential — a Type AC RCBO may not trip.

The result: live chassis, with no trip, no warning, no indication that anything is wrong.

**The fix costs Rs. 1,200–Rs. 2,500.**

That is the price difference between a Type AC RCBO and a Type A RCBO.

Type A detects both AC sinusoidal AND pulsating DC residual current. It will trip correctly when your solar inverter develops a fault. Type AC will not.

**This is not a theoretical risk.**

IEC 60755 (which defines RCBO types) specifically identifies pulsating DC leakage from switched-mode power supplies and power electronics as the hazard that Type A was designed to address. Solar inverters, VFDs, EV onboard chargers, and modern washing machine motors with variable-speed drives all produce this type of leakage.

Pakistan has installed:
- Millions of solar inverters (residential: 5kW hybrid most common)
- Growing EV charger installations (7kW AC home chargers)
- Widespread VFD use in industrial and agricultural tubewell applications

Every one of these applications requires Type A RCBO — not Type AC.
