**Why Pakistani electrical panels fail — and why it's almost always the busbar sizing**

I've seen it dozens of times. A panel is built, commissioned, and running fine. Then comes June. Lahore ambient hits 48°C. The switchroom with no cooling reaches 55°C. Within 3 months, a busbar joint starts glowing.

The panel builder followed the IEC table. He selected 50×5 mm copper busbar — rated at 430A. The load is 380A. That's 88% utilisation. Perfectly fine by European standards.

But IEC ampacity tables assume 35°C ambient. At 55°C, the de-rating factor is 0.71. Your 430A busbar can only carry 305A safely. At 380A load, it's running at 125% of its de-rated capacity.

The joint fails. Insurance doesn't cover "inadequate specification for site conditions." The factory loses production. The panel builder loses the contract.

---

**The fix is simple — and free:**

Pakistan ambient de-rating table for copper busbar:

| Ambient | De-rating | 50×5 mm Capacity |
|---------|-----------|-----------------|
| 35°C (IEC standard) | 1.00 | 430A |
| 40°C | 0.94 | 404A |
| 45°C | 0.87 | 374A |
| 50°C | 0.79 | 340A |
| 55°C | 0.71 | 305A |

Always size your busbar for the worst-case summer ambient in the actual installation location. For a 380A panel load in a hot switchroom, go to 63×6 mm copper (505A de-rated at 45°C). The extra Rs 15,000 in copper bar is nothing versus a panel failure.

**For factories above 630 kVA — consider busbar trunking:**

When transformer sizes pass 630 kVA or distribution runs exceed 10 metres, individual busbars become impractical. Sandwich busbar trunking (BBT) at Rs 4,500–45,000/metre delivers:
→ Plug-in tap points anywhere along the run
→ IP54 protection against Karachi coastal dust and humidity
→ Faster installation vs. cable pulling
→ Built-in short-circuit withstand rating
