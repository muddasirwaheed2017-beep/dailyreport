## Post

**We audited 30+ factory electricity bills in Pakistan. The same mistake kept appearing.**

The factory had a three-phase supply. Industrial B-2 tariff. Monthly bill in the Rs 400,000–800,000 range.

And a basic Class 2 digital energy meter as the "check meter."

Here is the problem: Class 2 means ±2% error is acceptable under IEC 62053-21. On a Rs 600,000 bill, that is up to Rs 12,000/month systematic inaccuracy — and billing meters are calibrated to the high end of their error band.

The factory had no way to dispute anything because they had no calibrated reference data.

---

**The fix takes three things:**

1. **Right IEC class for your application:**
 • Apartment sub-metering → Class 2 (Rs 2,800–4,500)
 • Factory B-2/B-3 check meter → Class 1 (Rs 8,500–28,000)
 • Solar net metering audit meter → Class 0.5S (Rs 18,000–55,000)

2. **Right CT ratio:** Full Load Current × 1.2 safety factor, then pick the next standard CT size (100/5, 150/5, 200/5... up to 2000/5 A). Using a Class 1 CT with a Class 0.5S meter cancels the meter's accuracy advantage.

3. **Right communication:** If you want logged data for dispute evidence, you need RS-485 Modbus output — not a manual-read display.

---

**The NEPRA angle no one explains:**

Under NEPRA's 2025 revised net metering framework, your DISCo's meter is the billing reference. But NEPRA explicitly allows you to install your own Class 0.5S bi-directional check meter. If the two meters diverge by more than 1.5%, you have grounds for a formal NEPRA dispute.

Commercial solar owners who installed check meters in 2025 recovered creditable export kWh they had been losing silently for months.

---

**The modern factory metering stack:**

Main incomer → Class 0.5S check meter
Feeders → Class 1 DIN rail Modbus sub-meters
All meters → RS-485 bus → data logger → energy dashboard

This architecture:

✅ Creates the ISO 50001 Significant Energy Use data trail automatically
