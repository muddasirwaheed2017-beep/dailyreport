## The question Pakistani farmers keep getting wrong about solar tubewells

Every week we see the same mistake on project sites across Pakistan:

A farmer invests Rs 4–6 lakh in solar panels for his tubewell. The installer sizes the PV array at 1:1 with the motor nameplate kW.

The pump runs well from 11am to 2pm.

The rest of the day? Insufficient voltage. Underperforming MPPT. The pump crawls or cuts out.

After one season the farmer concludes: "Solar doesn't work for tubewells."

The panels are fine. The array sizing rule is wrong.

---

**The correct rule is 1.3–1.5 × motor kW.**

A 5 HP (3.7 kW) AC tubewell motor needs 5–5.5 kWp of panels — not 3.7 kWp.

The extra capacity covers MPPT converter losses and gives the controller enough headroom to start the pump earlier in the morning, keep it running through passing clouds, and deliver meaningful output until late afternoon.

That additional 1.3–1.5 kWp costs maybe Rs 15,000–25,000 extra in panels. The additional water pumped per season — at the diesel price you're no longer paying — pays for that difference in weeks.

---

**AC VFD or DC controller? Most Pakistani projects should choose the VFD route.**

An MPPT solar pump VFD converts the solar array's DC output into variable-frequency AC your existing motor can use. You keep the AC motor in the borehole — the motor that any local winder can rewind, any electrician can test, any spare-parts shop stocks overloads for.

A DC solar pump controller drives a special BLDC pump. It is slightly more efficient at small scales (under 3 HP), but the motor is harder to source, harder to repair, and harder to find a local electrician who knows it.

For anything 5 HP and above, the AC VFD + changeover (solar by day, WAPDA/generator backup) is the dominant configuration in Pakistan for good reason: it integrates with the existing grid supply without a new motor investment.

---

**The four protections you cannot compromise on:**

1. **Dry-run / no-water protection** — Pakistan's water tables are dropping. Without it, a dry borehole burns the motor in minutes.
2. **Over/under-voltage** — rural feeder voltage swings by ±20% are normal. The controller must tolerate this.
3. **Reverse polarity and surge protection** on the DC input — field arrays attract lightning. One spike without protection destroys the MPPT stage.
4. **Overload and over-temperature** — the controller should derate gracefully in 45°C+ Pakistani summer heat, not fail.

---

**On NEPRA and approvals:**

A standalone solar tubewell that does not export power to the grid requires no NEPRA net-metering approval. You simply power your own pump. Keep a manual changeover to WAPDA as backup and the system is regulatory-clean.

If you're building a grid-tied hybrid that can export — that's when net-metering and DISCO approval apply.
