What is Electrical Conduit?

Electrical conduit is a tube or channel that encloses and protects electrical wiring from mechanical damage, moisture, UV radiation, fire, and electromagnetic interference. In Pakistani electrical installations, conduit serves four critical functions:

  1. Mechanical protection: Protects cables from construction damage, rodent attack (a significant issue in Pakistan's agricultural and storage environments), and physical impact in industrial settings.
  2. Cable replacement without wall breaking: Cables can be withdrawn and replaced without breaking plaster — critical when Pakistani voltage fluctuations and load-shedding cycling degrade cable insulation prematurely.
  3. Fire containment: GI conduit prevents fire spread through cable routes in commercial and industrial buildings — required by NFPA 70 and local Pakistan fire codes.
  4. EMC shielding: GI conduit provides Faraday shielding for sensitive signal and control wiring adjacent to power cables — particularly important in VFD installations common in Pakistan's textile and cement industry.

Why Pakistan Makes Conduit Selection Critical — 5 Local Factors

1. Pakistan UV and Heat Degradation of PVC Conduit

Standard PVC conduit (IEC 61386 Class 3221) is rated to 60°C continuous. In Pakistan's peak summer (May–July), ambient temperatures in Karachi, Multan, Faisalabad, and Sukkur regularly reach 45–50°C. South-facing exterior walls absorb direct solar radiation and reach surface temperatures of 65–75°C — exceeding the standard PVC conduit's thermal rating by 5–15°C.

At temperatures above rating, PVC conduit undergoes accelerated UV oxidation (if UV-unstabilised), losing tensile strength and impact resistance. Within 3–5 years, standard white PVC conduit on Pakistani rooftops or south walls becomes brittle, yellows, develops surface crazing, and eventually cracks — exposing the enclosed cables. The failure mode is particularly dangerous when the cracked conduit is on a live roof (solar panels, AC outdoor units) where regular inspection is unlikely.

Pakistan specification rule: Any conduit run that will be exposed to direct sunlight, on a rooftop, or on a south- or west-facing wall — specify heavy-duty UV-stabilised PVC (grey, Class 4 UV) or GI conduit. The price premium for UV-resistant PVC (Rs. 110–140/m vs Rs. 45–55/m for standard 20mm) is fully justified by a 15-year service life vs 3–5 years for standard PVC in the same location.

2. Load-Shedding Thermal Cycling — Expansion Stress

In Europe or North America, an electrical installation undergoes 1–2 significant thermal cycles per day. In Pakistan, load-shedding imposes 3–12 generator start-stop cycles per day (varying by region and schedule), each cycling the generator switchgear, changeover switch, and main panel from ambient to operating temperature and back. For conduit runs longer than 10m, this thermal cycling creates significant linear expansion: PVC expands at 0.07mm/m/°C, so a 15m PVC conduit run cycling from 30°C to 65°C during load-shedding operation expands by 37mm end-to-end.

Standard saddle-clipped conduit without expansion couplings cannot accommodate this movement, creating stress at junction boxes and panel entry points. The fix: install expansion couplings every 10–12m on any outdoor PVC conduit run, reduce saddle spacing to 600mm maximum (vs 900mm standard) for outdoor hot runs, and use flexible conduit sections at panel entries and terminal boxes.

3. WAPDA Commercial / Industrial Compliance Requirements

WAPDA's commercial and industrial metering regulations (as implemented by all four DISCOs — LESCO, IESCO, HESCO, GEPCO) specify GI conduit for the service entry conduit from the metering point to the main incoming isolation device. Key compliance points:

  • GI conduit (minimum 25mm) from WAPDA metering cabinet to incoming isolation switch — PVC not accepted
  • Conduit must be securely fixed and tamper-evident to prevent meter tampering
  • All conduit entries into the metering cabinet must be sealed with conduit bushings (fire-stop sealant at fire-rated walls)
  • GI conduit continuity must be maintained (no unbranded flexible inserts between GI sections)
  • Outdoor metering kiosk conduit entry must be weatherproof (IP54 minimum — achieved with weatherproof hoods or liquid-tight conduit entry fittings)

Failing WAPDA inspection on conduit specification is one of the most common reasons commercial connections in Pakistan are delayed — sometimes by months awaiting re-inspection appointments.

4. Flexible Conduit at Motor Terminal Boxes — The Vibration Problem

The single most common conduit failure mode in Pakistani industrial installations is rigid conduit fracture at the motor terminal box connection. Rigid PVC or GI conduit is fixed to a building structure; the motor vibrates on its baseplate. Any vibration transmission path — belt drive, direct coupling, pump cavitation — creates micro-movement at the conduit-to-terminal-box joint. Over 12–24 months of continuous operation, this micro-movement work-hardens the conduit material at the joint, causing fatigue cracks.

The consequence is cable exposure at the motor terminal — a live electrical hazard in a wet or dusty environment. The solution is a flexible conduit section (minimum 1.0m, recommended 1.5m) between the last fixed conduit saddle and the motor terminal box. This section absorbs all vibration without transmitting it to the fixed conduit. In Pakistan's pump-intensive agriculture and industrial sector (tubewell pumps, process pumps, compressors), this is not optional — it is the difference between a 2-year installation and a 15-year installation.

5. Conduit Fill Ratio and Pakistan Thermal De-rating

The IEC/BS maximum conduit fill ratio is 40% of the conduit's internal cross-sectional area for three or more cables. At 40% fill, cables are in contact with each other and rely on the conduit material to conduct heat away. In Pakistan's 45°C ambient environment, cables in a 40%-filled conduit at nominal current already operate at their rated temperature limit. Any current above nominal — which is commonplace in Pakistan due to voltage fluctuations (185–250V single phase vs rated 230V) causing over-current — immediately pushes cable insulation above rated temperature.

The Pakistan engineering practice: size conduits for 35% maximum fill, and for long runs (over 10m) or runs in hot roof spaces, reduce to 30%. This apparently conservative approach prevents premature cable insulation degradation that produces the fire and fault risk familiar to Pakistan's electrical maintenance industry.

Types of Conduit Available in Pakistan

PVC Conduit

  • Light-duty (Class 3111): Thin-wall, lowest cost. Suitable only for concealed wiring in low-load domestic installations. Not suitable for any surface-mounted or external run in Pakistan. Bends easily with spring bender.
  • Medium-duty (Class 3221): Standard domestic and commercial grade. Most common in Pakistan. 60°C rating. White or grey. Suitable for concealed interior runs.
  • Heavy-duty UV-stabilised (Class 4321/4421): Thicker wall, UV stabiliser added, grey colour. 80°C+ rating. Required for exterior, rooftop, and exposed runs in Pakistan. Carries 12–15 year outdoor service life expectation vs 3–5 years for standard.

GI (Galvanized Iron) Conduit

  • Electrogalvanized: Thin zinc coating (15–25µm). Adequate for dry indoor industrial environments. Corrodes in 5–8 years in Karachi coastal air.
  • Hot-dip galvanized (HDG): Heavy zinc coat (85–100µm). Required for Karachi coastal, outdoor exposed, and buried applications. Available from specialist suppliers in Pakistan's industrial areas.
  • Black (ungalvanized): Painted steel. Lowest cost GI conduit. Indoor only — corrodes rapidly if moisture contact. Occasionally sold in Pakistan as "GI conduit" — verify galvanizing before specification.

Flexible Conduit

  • Standard PVC-coated: Galvanized steel interlocked core with PVC sheath. For dry indoor motor connections. IP40. Most common in Pakistan for domestic/light industrial motors.
  • Liquid-tight (LFMC): Galvanized steel core with PVC jacket sealed to prevent liquid ingress. IP65–IP67. Required for outdoor pumps, tubewell connections, food processing, car wash, and chemical plant in Pakistan.
  • Stainless steel (SS316): For food, pharmaceutical, and coastal installations. Highest cost. Negligible corrosion in 20+ years.

Conduit Installation — Pakistan Standard Practice

  1. Plan the route before cutting: Mark conduit path on wall; measure and pre-cut to length with hacksaw or pipe cutter (PVC) or pipe cutter/hacksaw (GI). Add 20% for bends and overlaps.
  2. Deburr all cut ends: Use reamer or file to remove all internal burrs. Sharp edges cut cable insulation during pull — especially critical for VFD-output screened cables with thin outer insulation.
  3. Chase wall or fix saddles: For concealed: chase to conduit OD + 5mm clearance. For surface: fix saddles at 600mm spacing (horizontal), 900mm (vertical). Tighten to fix conduit but allow movement — saddles that grip too tightly prevent thermal expansion.
  4. Install expansion couplings: Add expansion coupling every 10–12m on outdoor or exposed runs. Critical for Pakistani summer heat. Slide the coupling half-engaged at installation to allow thermal growth in both directions.
  5. Install junction boxes at turns: Pull-point junction boxes at every second change-of-direction. In Pakistani construction, attempting to pull cable through long conduit runs with multiple bends without pull-points accounts for the majority of cable damage during installation.
  6. Install flexible section at motors: Final 1.0–1.5m to every motor terminal box. Fix the last rigid conduit saddle securely; the flexible section runs free to absorb vibration. Seal flexible conduit entry to motor terminal box with correct fitting (liquid-tight fitting for IP55 motors).
  7. Seal panel entries: Use conduit locknut inside the panel and conduit bushing (plastic or metal). Apply fire-stop sealant at all conduit penetrations through fire-rated walls or floors.
  8. Pull cable: Use fish tape to pull draw wire through first; then pull cables with draw wire using cable pulling lubricant for conduit fill over 25%. Never exceed 40% fill (35% in Pakistan — thermal de-rating). Do not exceed manufacturer's maximum pulling tension for the cable (typically 50N/mm² of conductor cross-section).

6 Common Conduit Mistakes in Pakistan

  1. Standard white PVC conduit outdoors: Fastest path to a cracked, exposed-cable hazard. UV and heat degrade standard PVC in 3–5 years on Pakistani rooftops. Use heavy-duty UV-stabilised PVC or GI.
  2. No flexible conduit at motor terminal boxes: Rigid conduit-to-terminal-box connections fracture within 12–24 months. A 1.5m flexible section at every motor is the single most cost-effective maintenance reduction in Pakistan's industrial electrical installations.
  3. PVC conduit for WAPDA commercial entry: WAPDA/DISCO inspectors reject PVC at metering point. Causes multi-month connection delays. GI is the only accepted material for commercial metering conduit in Pakistan.
  4. Overfilled conduit (>40% fill): Cables overheat in Pakistani summer heat. Standard cable thermal ratings assume 30°C ambient; in Pakistan's 45°C ambient, de-rate to 35% fill maximum in conduit runs.
  5. No expansion coupling on long outdoor runs: 15m PVC outdoor run in Pakistan expands up to 37mm between summer and winter. Without expansion couplings, stress causes junction box cracking and conduit saddle pull-out.
  6. Rigid conduit through vibrating equipment: Any conduit to pump, compressor, generator, or vibrating equipment without a flexible conduit section will develop fatigue cracks at the equipment connection within 12–24 months of operation.

What is the price of 20mm PVC conduit per metre in Pakistan 2026?

Standard 20mm PVC conduit costs Rs. 45–55/m (budget), Rs. 65–80/m (mid-range), and Rs. 110–140/m (heavy-duty UV-resistant) in Pakistan in 2026. Budget conduit is suitable for concealed indoor work; UV-resistant heavy-duty is required for outdoor or rooftop runs in Pakistan's 45–50°C summer heat.

Which conduit is required for WAPDA commercial connections in Pakistan?

WAPDA commercial and industrial metering connections require GI conduit from the service entry point to the main distribution board. PVC conduit is not accepted at the metering point for commercial connections. GI conduit 20mm costs Rs. 110–140/m (standard galvanized), Rs. 180–220/m (mid-range), and Rs. 280–320/m (heavy galvanized) in Pakistan 2026.

Why does PVC conduit crack in Pakistani summers?

Standard PVC conduit is rated to 60°C. In Pakistan's summers (45–50°C ambient), south-facing exterior walls reach 65–75°C surface temperature, pushing standard PVC beyond its rating. UV radiation accelerates polymer degradation — conduit becomes brittle within 3–5 years on exposed outdoor runs. The fix: use heavy-duty UV-resistant PVC (rated 80°C+) for outdoor/rooftop runs, or switch to GI conduit. Never use standard white PVC conduit in direct sunlight in Pakistan.

Do I need flexible conduit for motor wiring in Pakistan?

Yes — a minimum 1.0–1.5m flexible conduit section at every motor terminal box is required. Rigid conduit transmits vibration stresses to the conduit-to-box connection, causing fractures within 12–24 months in typical Pakistani pump and motor installations. Flexible PVC-coated conduit costs Rs. 90–110/m (standard) and Rs. 150–180/m (liquid-tight) in Pakistan 2026. Liquid-tight is recommended for outdoor motors, tubewell pumps, and food/chemical industry.

What is the maximum conduit fill ratio allowed in Pakistan panels?

The IEC/BS standard maximum fill ratio is 40% for three or more cables in a conduit. In Pakistan, the practical recommendation is 35% maximum due to higher ambient temperatures (45°C+). A 25mm PVC conduit (ID ~21mm, area ~346mm²) should carry cables totalling no more than 121mm² combined cross-section area.

What flexible conduit size is needed for a 5.5kW motor in Pakistan?

A 5.5kW (7.5HP) three-phase motor draws approximately 12A full-load current, requiring 4mm² cable minimum. For standard cable, 25mm flexible conduit is adequate. For VFD output with screened cable (larger OD), use 32mm flexible conduit. Flexible conduit 25mm costs Rs. 110–140/m (standard) and Rs. 195–235/m (liquid-tight) in Pakistan 2026.
