Phase Failure Relay Price Pakistan 2026 — Voltage Asymmetry, WAPDA Summer Sag & Motor Protection Buyer Guide

Voltage asymmetry is the number one cause of 3-phase motor burnout in Pakistan — and most thermal overload relays miss it entirely. When WAPDA's summer load pressure pushes one phase to 380 V while another sags to 340 V, the voltage asymmetry can exceed 5 %. At 5 % asymmetry, motor current unbalance reaches 25–50 %, rotor temperatures spike by 25 °C, and winding insulation begins to degrade. A thermal overload relay set correctly for full load current sees nothing unusual — until the motor has already been burning for months.

A phase failure relay (also called a voltage monitoring relay or 3-phase monitoring relay) watches voltage directly: it measures R-Y-B phase voltages, calculates asymmetry, and trips the contactor within 0.1–5 seconds when any threshold is crossed. This guide covers what to set, what to buy, what it costs in Pakistan 2026 (PKR), and the seven mistakes that make phase failure relay installations fail.

What Voltage Asymmetry Actually Does to a Motor

Three-phase motors are designed for balanced voltages — R, Y, and B within ±1 % of each other. In practice, Pakistan's WAPDA grid supplies industrial feeders at 380–415 V (3-phase) with asymmetry that varies from <1 % at night to 4–8 % during summer peak hours (June–September, 14:00–22:00).

The physics is counterintuitive: a small voltage unbalance produces a large current unbalance. The National Electrical Manufacturers Association (NEMA) rule of thumb: 1 % voltage asymmetry → 6–10 % current asymmetry. At 5 % voltage asymmetry, one winding can carry 30–50 % more current than the others. This is localised heating — the thermal overload relay, which averages across all three phases using a bimetal strip, misses the hot winding entirely.

WAPDA Summer Voltage Sag — What It Is and How to Set Your Relay

Pakistan's WAPDA summer peak (June–September) combines maximum industrial load, agricultural tubewell load, and air conditioning load. The result is a predictable daily voltage sag pattern on most industrial feeders:

  • Night (23:00–07:00): 400–415 V, asymmetry <1.5 %. Voltage is closest to specification.
  • Morning (07:00–12:00): 390–405 V, asymmetry 1–3 %. Acceptable for most motors.
  • Afternoon peak (12:00–22:00): 350–385 V on heavily loaded feeders, asymmetry 3–8 %. This is the damage window.

Asymmetry Trip Setting

Most phase failure relays offer asymmetry trip from 2 % to 10 %. For Pakistan, set 3 % for motors ≥11 kW and 5 % for motors ≤7.5 kW. Motors <7.5 kW are less sensitive to asymmetry damage because winding mass relative to current is higher.

Time Delay Setting

Set the trip time delay to 3–5 seconds (not zero). Why: WAPDA switching events during load shedding create momentary voltage dips that recover within 1–2 seconds. A zero-delay relay trips on every switching event — causing nuisance trips and wear on the contactor. A 3-second delay filters switching transients while still catching genuine sustained asymmetry. Exception: for motors already running hot (above 70 % rated temperature by thermostat), use 1-second delay.

Load-shedding interaction: In Pakistan, load-shedding returns with a surge — voltage may temporarily overshoot to 430–440 V for 2–3 cycles before settling. Set the over-voltage trip to 115 % of nominal (437 V) with a 5-second delay to catch sustained over-voltage while ignoring the return surge transient.

7 Pakistan Phase Failure Relay Installation Mistakes (With PKR Repair Costs)

  1. Installing on the load side of the contactor. The relay must measure supply voltage, not motor back-EMF. Load-side connection gives a false voltage reading during motor coast-down and misses supply faults entirely. PKR repair cost if caught late: PKR 3,000–12,000 (motor rewind if asymmetry damage accumulates).
  2. Setting 5-second delay on a zero-delay relay output (using default). Most economy relays ship with a 0-second trip delay factory default. First WAPDA switching event (common in load-shedding areas) causes nuisance trip. Operator disables the relay entirely. PKR consequence: relay renders worthless after first nuisance trip. PKR 1,200–3,000 for relay replacement + rewiring time.
  3. Using a phase-loss-only relay and assuming it catches asymmetry. A phase-loss relay only trips when one phase drops to zero (complete failure). Voltage asymmetry of 4 % — which causes 25 °C extra rotor heating — is invisible to it. PKR consequence: motor rewind (PKR 15,000–80,000 depending on kW) after 2–3 Pakistan summers.
  4. Setting under-voltage threshold too low (below 85 %). Setting the trip at 300 V (79 %) instead of 330 V (87 %) lets the motor run at highly depressed voltage where torque is reduced by the square of voltage. A motor running at 300 V when it should cut out runs at 78 % of rated torque — it can stall on normal load, drawing locked-rotor current silently until the thermal OL trips (too late). PKR consequence: thermal OL trips, motor restarts, stalls again — winding insulation failure within weeks.
  5. Not connecting the relay output to the contactor coil control circuit. The relay trip output (NC contact) must be wired in series with the start/stop control circuit so that tripping the relay drops the contactor. Installers sometimes wire the relay and forget to connect its output, leaving the relay as a light display with no protective action. PKR consequence: full motor burnout with no protection.
  6. Using auto-restart on machine tools. As noted above: a lathe or grinder that auto-restarts after a load-shedding event can cause serious injury. PKR consequence: PSQCA and factory inspection fine PKR 25,000–150,000 + compensation liability. Use manual reset for all machines with rotating tooling.
  7. Single relay on multiple motors from one feeder. A single phase failure relay on the main incomer protects against supply faults at the feeder level, but not individual motor-level faults (single motor overloaded asymmetrically by unbalanced load). Each motor needs its own relay, or the incomer relay supplemented by per-motor thermal overloads. PKR consequence: PKR 8,000–45,000 per motor rewind for the unprotected motors.

Complete Installation Checklist (12-Point)

  1. ☐ Confirm supply voltage range and match to relay input range (380–415 V most common in Pakistan)
  2. ☐ Connect relay sensing terminals to supply side of contactor (not load side)
  3. ☐ Set under-voltage trip: 330–340 V for most Pakistan DOL motors
  4. ☐ Set over-voltage trip: 430–440 V (115 % of 380 V)
  5. ☐ Set asymmetry trip: 3 % for motors ≥11 kW; 5 % for motors ≤7.5 kW
  6. ☐ Set trip time delay: 3–5 seconds (filters WAPDA switching transients)
  7. ☐ Set return delay (hysteresis): 5–10 seconds (prevents re-start cycling on oscillating supply)
  8. ☐ Wire relay NC output contact in series with contactor coil control circuit
  9. ☐ Verify trip function: temporarily short one phase at relay input — relay must drop contactor within set delay
  10. ☐ Label relay settings on panel door: UV trip, OV trip, asymmetry %, time delay
  11. ☐ Set auto/manual reset per machine safety classification (manual for rotating tooling)
  12. ☐ Record baseline voltages per phase (R, Y, B) on commissioning day for future reference

Frequently Asked Questions (Phase Failure Relay Pakistan)

What is a phase failure relay and why do I need one in Pakistan?
A phase failure relay (voltage monitoring relay) measures all three supply voltages and trips the motor contactor if it detects phase loss, wrong phase sequence, under-voltage, over-voltage, or voltage asymmetry. In Pakistan, WAPDA summer voltage sag (June–September) commonly causes asymmetry of 3–8 % on industrial feeders, which standard thermal overload relays do not detect. Price: PKR 800–18,000.
What is voltage asymmetry and why is it dangerous?
Voltage asymmetry is the percentage difference between the highest and lowest phase voltages. Even 3 % voltage asymmetry causes 15–25 % current asymmetry, raising rotor temperature by 10–18 °C and reducing motor life by 25–35 %. Pakistan's summer industrial feeders routinely hit 3–5 % asymmetry during 14:00–22:00 peak hours.
What under-voltage trip setting should I use for WAPDA Pakistan?
Set at 330 V (87 % of 380 V) for most Pakistan DOL motor starters. For sensitive processes, use 340 V (89 %). Always add a 3–5 second time delay to filter WAPDA switching transients.
Should I use auto-restart or manual reset?
Use manual reset for any machine with rotating tooling (lathes, grinders, compressors). Use auto-restart for unattended pumps, cooling tower fans, and HVAC systems where loss of service causes process damage. In Pakistan, auto-restart with 10–20 second delay is standard for irrigation and chiller water pumps.
What is the difference between a phase failure relay and a thermal overload relay?
Thermal overload relays detect mechanical overload via average current; they miss voltage asymmetry, phase loss (quickly), and supply quality faults. Phase failure relays detect supply voltage faults but not mechanical overload. Both are required for complete motor protection.
How do I wire a phase failure relay into a motor starter?
Connect sensing terminals (L1, L2, L3) to the supply side of the main contactor. Wire the relay NC output in series with the contactor coil control circuit. Never connect sensing terminals to the motor side of the contactor.
What time delay avoids nuisance trips from load-shedding?
Set trip delay to 3–5 seconds. WAPDA switching events create dips that recover in 1–2 seconds. Also set return delay to 5–10 seconds so the relay waits for supply stabilisation before allowing restart.
How much does a phase failure relay cost in Pakistan 2026?
Basic phase-loss relay: PKR 800–1,500. Phase monitoring (loss + sequence): PKR 1,200–2,200. Full voltage monitoring (asymmetry + UV + OV): PKR 2,500–7,500. Multifunction with display: PKR 7,000–18,000. Best value for Pakistan 7.5–22 kW motors: PKR 3,500–5,500.
