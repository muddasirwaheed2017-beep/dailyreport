What Is a Cable Gland?

A cable gland (also called a cable connector, cable fitting, or strain relief fitting) is a mechanical device installed at the point where a cable enters an electrical enclosure — a panel board, junction box, distribution board, motor terminal box, or instrument housing. It performs four functions simultaneously:

  1. Sealing: prevents moisture, dust, and insects from entering the enclosure through the cable entry hole
  2. Strain relief: clamps the cable jacket and prevents tension on internal terminations
  3. EMC screening: on metal (brass) glands, provides a 360° bonded termination for the cable screen or armour
  4. Earth continuity: on armoured cable glands, provides the armour-to-earth bond required by IEC 60364 and WAPDA earthing rules

A cable gland has three main components: the body (threaded into the enclosure knockdown), the locknut (secures the body from inside), and the clamping nut (compresses the rubber seal around the cable). Armoured glands have an additional armour clamp ring and in some designs a shroud (insulating sleeve over the armour for second-end isolation).

Why Cable Glands Are Critical in Pakistan

1. Monsoon Ingress — The Number One Panel Killer

Pakistan's monsoon season (July–September) delivers rainfall intensities of 40–120mm per hour in Karachi, Lahore, and Islamabad. Poorly sealed cable entries are the leading cause of panel board insulation failures during and after monsoon. A single plastic PG-thread gland with a cracked rubber seal allows water to wick along the cable jacket and pool at the bottom of the panel — causing ground faults, MCB tripping, and busbar short-circuits. Specify IP66 minimum for any outdoor DB box; IP67 or IP68 for ground-level and low-wall installations in flood-prone areas (Karachi DHA, Lahore Model Town basement feeders, Islamabad F-sectors with high water tables).

2. WAPDA Armoured Cable Earthing Requirements

Under WAPDA's distribution standards and IEC 60364-5-54, all steel wire armoured (SWA) and steel tape armoured (STA) cables must have their armour earthed at both ends. The armoured cable gland is the mechanical means of achieving this. Non-compliant installations — where the armour floats or is earthed at one end only — pass WAPDA audits initially but fail when the armour picks up an induced voltage on long runs (typically >50m for 3-phase feeders). Use a Type E1W or E2W brass armoured gland and verify earth continuity (armour to panel earth bar <0.1Ω) during commissioning.

3. Karachi Coastal Corrosion — Brass vs Plastic

Karachi's coastal environment (average NaCl airborne concentration 0.05–0.8 mg/cm²/day within 10km of the sea) causes two failure modes: (a) dezincification of standard brass (CW614N) within 18–30 months — the gland body crumbles from the thread outward; (b) UV-induced embrittlement of non-stabilised nylon glands within 12–24 months of direct sun exposure. For Karachi installations within 30km of the coast: specify nickel-plated brass glands with dezincification-resistant (DZR) brass body (CW602N alloy) or 316 stainless steel. For inland Punjab and KPK: standard nickel-plated brass (CW614N) is adequate with 5–7 year life expectancy.

4. Load-Shedding Thermal Cycling — Cable Jacket Shrinkage

Pakistan's 6–12 hour daily load-shedding creates repeated thermal cycles — cables heat under load, then cool during outage. Over 2–3 years, cable jackets undergo thermal shrinkage (polyethylene jacket shrinks 1–3% longitudinally), pulling cables backward through the gland. This is the primary cause of cable strain at terminal blocks and busbar connections. Counter-measures: (a) use a gland with a double-grip clamping seal (two rubber sealing rings); (b) leave a 100–150mm service loop inside the panel before the first gland after entry.

5. PG vs Metric Thread Confusion in Pakistani Markets

The Pakistani electrical market stocks primarily PG-thread glands (German standard DIN 40430, also known as Panzer-Gewinde). New IEC-compliant panels specify metric ISO thread (M16, M20, M25…). The threads are NOT interchangeable — a PG13.5 gland will not thread into an M20 knockout and vice versa. Many panel builders order glands by cable size (mm²) rather than thread size, causing site-level mismatches. Always specify both the thread type and size AND the cable OD clamping range when ordering.

Types of Cable Glands — Complete Guide

Type 1: Standard Plastic (PA6 Nylon) — PG Thread

The most common cable gland in Pakistan. PA6 nylon withstands temperatures from -20°C to +80°C, is UV-stabilised (grey or black), and achieves IP54 (standard) to IP68 (with silicone seal). Used in: control panels, DB boxes, junction boxes, motor terminal boxes (indoor). Limitation: not suitable for chemical environments, temperatures above 90°C, or applications requiring EMC screening. Price: Rs 15–350 depending on size.

Type 2: Nickel-Plated Brass — PG or Metric Thread

The standard for outdoor and industrial applications in Pakistan. Brass body provides mechanical strength, EMC screening (360° bonded termination for cable screen), and high IP rating (IP66–IP68 with appropriate seal). Nickel plating provides corrosion resistance in normal environments (inland Pakistan). Suitable for: outdoor distribution boards, generator panels, street light panels, solar inverter connections, industrial MCC entry. Temperature rating: -40°C to +100°C. Price: Rs 80–1,200.

Type 3: Stainless Steel (SS316) — Metric Thread

Premium option for Karachi coastal, marine, chemical, and food-processing environments. SS316 (marine-grade stainless) resists chloride-induced corrosion that destroys standard brass within 18 months in Karachi sea air. IP68 rated as standard. Used in: port facilities (Karachi, Gwadar), desalination plants, chemical plants (Bin Qasim industrial zone), food processing, pharmaceutical clean rooms. Price: Rs 450–2,500 depending on size. 3–5× cost premium over brass is justified by 10–15 year service life vs 2–3 years for standard brass.

Type 4: Armoured Cable Gland (SWA/STA) — Type E1W / E2W

Designed specifically for steel wire armoured (SWA) and steel tape armoured (STA) cables. Provides: (a) inner seal on cable inner sheath, (b) armour clamp ring gripping the armour wires, (c) earth continuity from armour to enclosure, (d) outer seal on cable outer sheath. Type E1W: inner seal only (earth via armour clamp). Type E2W: inner + outer seal (used where outer sheath must be maintained intact). In Pakistan, E1W is most common. Used in: all armoured cable terminations — sub-distribution feeders, motor power cables, underground service entries, building main feeders. Price: Rs 450–2,800 by size.

Type 5: IP68 Multi-Hole / Membrane Glands

Multiple cables through one gland entry — typically 2, 3, 4, or 6 holes. Used where several small cables exit through a single knockout: sensor wiring, instrument cables, CCTV/data cables in outdoor enclosures. Each hole has an independent silicone seal maintaining IP68. Unused holes are sealed with blanking inserts. Price: Rs 350–900 for 2–6 hole variants. Less common in Pakistan but growing with instrumentation and building automation installations.

Type 6: IECEx / ATEX Hazardous Area Glands

Certified for use in explosive atmospheres — CNG stations, LPG bottling plants, oil refineries (Karachi, Attock), gas pipelines. Provide a flameproof or increased-safety barrier at cable entry. Must carry IECEx certificate (or ATEX for European-equipment imports). In Pakistan, IECEx is the applicable standard. Ex e (increased safety) and Ex d (flameproof) variants available. Price: Rs 3,500–15,000+ depending on size and certification. Do NOT substitute standard industrial glands in hazardous areas — this is a life-safety non-compliance.

Always measure the actual cable OD.

Installation Checklist — Cable Gland Fitting

Pre-Installation

  • ✅ Measure cable OD with calipers — do not estimate from conductor mm²
  • ✅ Verify thread type matches enclosure knockout (PG, metric, NPT)
  • ✅ Verify gland clamping range covers cable OD with ≥2mm headroom
  • ✅ For armoured cables: measure armour OD separately and check against gland armour range
  • ✅ For outdoor use: confirm IP rating ≥ IP66 (IP68 for flood-risk areas)
  • ✅ For coastal locations: confirm DZR brass or stainless steel specification
  • ✅ Confirm gland material is compatible with cable jacket material (some chemical-resistant cables require special seal compounds)

Installation Steps

  1. Prepare the entry hole: ensure knockout is clean, burr-free, and sized correctly (PG13.5 requires 20.5mm hole; M20 requires 20.0mm)
  2. Apply thread sealant (for IP67/68 applications): PTFE tape or liquid thread sealant on the male thread — 2–3 wraps
  3. Thread the body into the enclosure from outside. Hand-tighten first
  4. Thread the locknut from inside and tighten to face of enclosure (20–25 Nm for M20 brass)
  5. Thread cable through body, sealing ring, and clamping nut
  6. For SWA armoured glands: strip outer sheath to expose 15mm of armour wires. Fan armour wires over the armour cone. Compress with the backnut. Verify armour-to-gland continuity (<0.1Ω)
  7. Tighten the clamping nut until the sealing ring grips the cable jacket — it should not be possible to pull the cable by hand. Torque: PG11 = 3–4 Nm; PG16 = 4–5 Nm; M20 = 5–7 Nm; M25 = 7–9 Nm
  8. Do not over-tighten — distorting the rubber seal splits it and destroys the IP rating

Post-Installation Verification

  • ✅ Pull-test: cable must not move when pulled with 50N (5kg) axial force
  • ✅ IP verification: visually check that clamping nut is seated flush and rubber seal is not extruding unevenly
  • ✅ For SWA glands: Earth continuity test — armour to panel earth bar <0.1Ω
  • ✅ Torque mark: apply a witness mark (paint pen line) across gland body and clamping nut — confirms no subsequent loosening during service
  • ✅ Unused knockouts: seal with IP-rated blanking plugs (not plastic caps without seals)

6 Common Cable Gland Mistakes in Pakistan

  1. Wrong size by conductor mm², not cable OD: A 4mm² 3-core SWA cable has OD of 18–24mm including the armour — requires M25 armoured gland, not PG13.5. This is the most common ordering error in Pakistan.
  2. Plastic gland in outdoor application: PA6 nylon degrades in Karachi UV within 12 months. Specify brass for any panel in direct sun or weather-exposed position.
  3. PG gland in metric knockout: Cross-threading destroys the enclosure knockout and requires a reducer insert patch — adds cost and reduces IP reliability.
  4. Armour not earthed at both ends: Single-ended armour earthing creates an antenna for induced interference on long motor feeder cables, causing VFD and motor protection relay nuisance tripping. Always earth at both ends.
  5. No service loop inside panel: Cable jacket thermal shrinkage from load-shedding cycles pulls cables backward through glands over 2–3 years — without a 100mm service loop, the terminal block connections pull loose.
  6. Standard gland in IECEx hazardous area: Using a non-certified gland in a Zone 1 or Zone 2 hazardous area is an insurance-void and life-safety violation. No exceptions for "temporary" or "low-voltage" circuits in hazardous areas.

What is the price of cable glands in Pakistan 2026?

Prices vary by brand, material, IP rating, and thread type. Stainless steel and IECEx-certified hazardous area glands carry a 3–8× premium over standard brass.

What is the difference between PG and metric cable glands?

PG (Panzer-Gewinde) thread is a German standard widely used in Pakistan's industrial market — PG7, PG9, PG11, PG13.5, PG16, PG21, PG29, PG36, PG42. Metric ISO thread (M16, M20, M25, M32, M40, M50) is the international IEC standard increasingly replacing PG in new panels. NPT (National Pipe Thread) is a US standard rarely used in Pakistan except in imported equipment. Most Pakistani panel builders stock PG-thread glands; new projects increasingly specify metric for IEC compliance. The threads are NOT interchangeable — never force a PG gland into a metric knockout.

Which cable gland IP rating is needed for outdoor use in Pakistan?

For outdoor distribution boxes (DB boxes) in Pakistan: minimum IP66 (dust-tight, high-pressure water jets). During monsoon season (July–September), IP67 (immersion up to 1m for 30 minutes) is recommended for any installation that may flood. For underground entries or permanently submerged cable entries (pumps, water features), specify IP68. For Karachi coastal areas, choose marine-grade brass or 316 stainless steel glands with IP66 minimum to resist salt air corrosion.

Do armoured cable glands require earthing in Pakistan?

Yes. Under WAPDA and IEC 60364 earthing requirements, the armour (steel wire or steel tape) of armoured cables must be earthed at both ends. Armoured cable glands achieve this through a continuous earth bond between the armour and the equipment enclosure. In Pakistan, single-ended earthing is sometimes seen in older installations — this is non-compliant and eliminates the fault-current return path. Always use a certified Type E1W or E2W armoured cable gland and verify continuity between armour and panel earth bar after installation (<0.1Ω).

How do I select the correct cable gland size?

Cable gland sizing is based on the cable's overall outer diameter (OD), not the conductor size. Step 1: measure the cable OD with calipers (or use manufacturer datasheet). Step 2: select a gland whose clamping range covers that OD with at least 1mm headroom on each side. Step 3: verify the thread entry matches your enclosure knockout (PG, metric, or NPT). Step 4: for armoured cables, ensure the gland's armour clamping range covers the armour diameter. Common mistake: ordering gland size by conductor mm² — a 4mm² cable may have OD 9–13mm depending on insulation, requiring PG11, PG13.5, or PG16.

What is the best cable gland material for Pakistan's climate?

Pakistan climate recommendation: (1) Indoor panels — PA6 nylon plastic (IP54, cost-effective for 35–45°C ambient). (2) Outdoor and industrial — nickel-plated brass (IP66–IP68, handles 50–55°C Pakistani summer). (3) Karachi coastal / chemical — 316 stainless steel or DZR brass. (4) Hazardous areas (refinery, CNG station) — IECEx/ATEX-certified brass or stainless steel. Avoid plain (non-nickel) brass within 30km of the Karachi coast — salt air causes dezincification within 18–30 months.
