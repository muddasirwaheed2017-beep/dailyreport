How an AC Magnetic Contactor Works

A magnetic contactor is an electrically operated switch. When you energise the coil (your control circuit — typically at 220V AC from a push-button or PLC output), the electromagnet pulls the armature, closing the main contacts (3 poles for a 3-phase motor). When the coil is de-energised — by a stop button, overload trip, or loss of control power — a return spring opens the contacts immediately. This fail-safe behaviour is the critical difference between a contactor and a manual switch: lose power, and the motor stops automatically.

Every contactor also has auxiliary contacts (typically 1 NO + 1 NC), used for: control circuit interlocking (star-delta changeover logic), run indicator lamps, PLC feedback, and electrical hold-in circuits (self-latching).

Star-Delta Starter — Contactor Sizing (Key Difference)

In a Star-Delta starter, the three contactors carry different current levels. Getting this wrong wastes money or causes premature failure:

  • Main contactor (KM1): Carries line current during delta run. Size at 58% of motor FLC (phase current = line current ÷ √3). Example: 22 kW motor FLC 45A → main contactor = 26A → use 32A AC3.
  • Star contactor (KM3): Carries only 33% of FLC for the brief starting period. Size at 33% of motor FLC. Example: 22 kW → 15A → use 18A AC3.
  • Delta contactor (KM2): Carries phase current during run. Same sizing as main contactor = 58% of FLC. Example: 22 kW → 26A → use 32A AC3.
  • Overload relay: Fits in the motor branch between KM1 and motor terminals. Set to 58% of FLC (not full FLC) because the OLR measures phase current, not line current.

Pakistan-Specific Considerations — WAPDA Voltage and Phase Issues

Pakistan's 3-phase WAPDA supply creates four specific hazards that your contactor and overload relay selection must account for:

  1. Voltage variation (380–440V): WAPDA 3-phase nominal is 400V ±10% (360–440V). Select contactors with AC3 rating confirmed at 440V. Check the contactor's AC3 rated operational current at 440V, not just 400V — some budget contactors derate significantly at higher voltage.
  2. Phase failure (single-phasing): One of the most common motor-killers in Pakistan. A blown fuse on one phase, a loose connection, or a feeder fault leaves a motor running on 2 phases — it continues to rotate but draws 170–200% current on remaining phases. Thermal OLRs with differential sensitivity will trip; basic thermal OLRs may not. For motors above 11 kW or process-critical applications, use a dedicated phase-failure relay or electronic OLR.
  3. Phase sequence variation: During WAPDA outages and generator switchover, phase sequence can reverse, causing motors to run backwards. This is catastrophic for centrifugal pumps (reverse flow, impeller damage). Use a phase-sequence relay alongside your contactor if operating on both WAPDA and generator. Your automatic changeover switch may include phase-sequence protection — check the specification.
  4. Voltage dips during starting: DOL starting a large motor on a weak rural WAPDA feeder causes a momentary voltage dip that can cause other equipment on the same feeder to trip. If the voltage dip exceeds 15%, WAPDA may impose a Star-Delta or soft-starter requirement. Urban industrial feeders are generally stronger and tolerate DOL up to 15 kW.

Motor Protection Stack — Complete Circuit for Pakistan

A properly protected motor installation in Pakistan needs four layers of protection, each addressing a different failure mode:

  1. Short-circuit protection — 3-pole MCB (C-curve) or MCCB upstream of the contactor, sized at 200–250% of motor FLC to ride through starting inrush without nuisance tripping.
  2. Switching and fail-safe control — AC magnetic contactor (main topic of this guide), sized at AC3 rated current ≥ motor FLC. Drops out on control power loss, stop command, or OLR trip.
  3. Overload and single-phasing protection — Thermal OLR or electronic motor protection relay, set at 100–115% of nameplate FLC. Provides time-delayed trip on sustained overload plus (model-dependent) differential protection for phase failure.
  4. Phase sequence and surge protection — Phase-sequence relay (PKR 800–2,500) prevents reverse rotation after changeover. For outdoor rural installations, add a Type 2 SPD surge protection device at the incomer during monsoon season.

Pre-Purchase Checklist — 10 Points Before Buying

  1. Read the motor nameplate: confirm HP/kW, voltage (400V or 220V), full-load amps, and frequency (50 Hz Pakistan).
  2. Identify the load type: AC3 for all standard induction motors in Pakistan (pumps, fans, compressors).
  3. Size the contactor using the AC3 column: select current rating ≥ motor FLC. Never use AC1 ratings for motors.
  4. Size the overload relay range: the adjustment dial must cover the motor's nameplate FLC. Set at 100–110% of FLC.
  5. Specify the coil voltage: confirm your control circuit voltage. Ordering the wrong coil voltage is the single most common installation error.
  6. For Star-Delta starters: order 3 contactors (main + star + delta). Size main and delta at 58% of FLC, star at 33% of FLC. Add a 5–15 second timer relay.
  7. Check the auxiliary contacts count: ensure the contactor has enough auxiliary contacts for your interlock and indicator circuit.
  8. Verify IEC 60947-4-1 certification marking on the contactor body and on the overload relay.
  9. For rural tubewell installations: add a phase-failure relay (PKR 800–2,500). Motor rewind costs PKR 15,000–80,000.
  10. Install inside an IP54-rated enclosure minimum — IP65 for outdoor agricultural panels exposed to Pakistan's monsoon dust and rain.

Frequently Asked Questions — Contactor and Overload Relay Pakistan

Q: What is the price of an AC contactor in Pakistan in 2026?
A: PKR 800–1,600 for a 9A contactor (up to 5.5 HP); PKR 6,000–11,000 for a 95A contactor (up to 60 HP). IEC-certified models cost 20–35% more than non-certified equivalents but carry a rated service life of 1 million AC3 operations.

Q: What size contactor do I need for a 5 HP tubewell pump?
A: A 5 HP (3.7 kW) 3-phase motor at 400V draws approximately 8A FLC. Select an 18A AC3 contactor (provides headroom for starting inrush without contact stress). Pair with a 5.5–8A thermal overload relay set at 7.5–8A.

Q: What is the difference between DOL and Star-Delta starter?
A: DOL applies full voltage directly (1 contactor, simple, high starting current). Star-Delta reduces starting current to 33% of DOL value using 3 contactors + timer (more complex, lower grid impact). Use Star-Delta for motors above 10 HP or on weak rural WAPDA feeders.

Q: Why is my contactor chattering?
A: Most likely WAPDA voltage dropping below the contactor's minimum pick-up voltage (85% of rated coil voltage). Check supply voltage at the control panel. Other causes: worn shading ring, loose wiring, wrong coil voltage.

Q: Can I use one contactor for a 3-phase motor?
A: Yes for DOL — 1 three-pole (TP) contactor plus 1 overload relay is all that's needed. Never use a single-phase or 2-pole contactor on a 3-phase motor — single-phasing will destroy the motor.
