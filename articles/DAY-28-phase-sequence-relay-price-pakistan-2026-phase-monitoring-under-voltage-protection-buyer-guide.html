What is a Phase Sequence Relay and What Does It Protect Against?

A phase sequence relay (PSR) is a voltage-sensing device wired to all three supply phases. It continuously monitors the incoming supply and cuts power to the motor (via a contactor) when it detects a harmful condition. Depending on the model, protection can include:

  • Phase reversal — wrong phase order (R-B-Y instead of R-Y-B) causes motors to run backwards, destroying pumps, compressors, and gearboxes
  • Phase loss (open phase / single-phasing) — one line goes dead but the motor continues on two phases, drawing 173% rated current; motor burns within minutes
  • Under-voltage — WAPDA low voltage causes motors to draw excess current and overheat; particularly dangerous during summer peak load and in rural feeder areas
  • Over-voltage — rare on WAPDA but occurs after load shedding restoration; damages motor winding insulation
  • Phase asymmetry / unbalance — different voltages on the three phases cause negative sequence currents; equivalent to a derating of the motor

Important distinction: A basic phase sequence relay only catches phase reversal. It will NOT save your motor from phase loss or low voltage. For Pakistan, always buy a relay with at least phase loss + phase reversal + under-voltage protection.

Why Phase Protection Is Critical for Pakistan

Pakistani WAPDA supply has unique characteristics that make phase monitoring non-optional for any industrial or agricultural installation:

  • Routine phase loss: WAPDA scheduled load shedding often restores power with one or two phases missing for 5–30 seconds before the third phase reconnects. A motor that restarts single-phased burns in under 3 minutes.
  • Phase reversal after rewiring: Every time WAPDA maintenance crews reconnect lines after a fault or upgrade, there is a ~30% chance the phase sequence at the consumer end has reversed. No external sign — the motor simply runs backwards.
  • Low voltage endemic: NTDC data shows that rural 11kV feeders in Punjab and Sindh regularly deliver 320–350V line-to-line (80–87% of nominal 400V) during evening peak. Motors running at 330V draw 17% excess current and run 15°C hotter — shortening winding insulation life by 50%.
  • Summer ambient temperatures: Pakistan's 40–48°C summers already stress motor thermal limits. Any electrical imbalance on top of peak ambient makes burnout nearly certain without phase protection.

Net result: in Pakistan, a phase monitoring relay is not a luxury — it is a mandatory component of any 3-phase motor installation, from a 1 HP tubewell to a 100 HP industrial compressor.

Types of Phase Protection Relays Available in Pakistan

Type 1 — Basic Phase Sequence Relay (PSR)

Detects wrong phase order only. Output: 1 relay contact. Power consumption: <2W. No adjustment needed — factory set for 380–420V three-phase. Smallest and cheapest. Sufficient only for installations where phase loss is impossible (e.g., behind a reliable three-phase UPS or generator with fixed sequence).

Type 2 — Phase Loss + Sequence Relay

Detects phase reversal AND open phase (single-phasing). Still no voltage monitoring. Good for installations where voltage is stable (generator supply) but phase wiring quality is uncertain.

Type 3 — Phase Monitoring Relay (Full Protection)

The standard specification for any Pakistani WAPDA-connected motor. Monitors: phase sequence, phase loss, under-voltage, over-voltage, phase asymmetry. Typically includes adjustable trip thresholds and trip delay (0.1–30 seconds). Most models also provide a fault-type indication LED or output signal. This is the recommended type for Pakistan.

Type 4 — Adjustable Digital Phase Monitoring Relay

Adds digital setpoint entry, wide voltage range (100–480V 3-phase), and separate trip delay setting for each fault type. More expensive but justified for critical loads: compressors, irrigation pumps in remote areas, and industrial machinery where nuisance trips are costly.

Type 5 — Motor Protection Relay (MPR) / Electronic Overload Relay

A full motor protection relay (MPR) adds current-based protection on top of voltage-based protection: thermal overload, phase unbalance in current, earth fault, locked rotor detection, thermistor input. Replaces both the bimetal overload relay and the phase monitoring relay in one unit. Best for motors above 22 kW or in critical processes where downtime is expensive.

How to Wire a Phase Monitoring Relay (Step-by-Step)

Wiring a phase monitoring relay is straightforward. The relay does not interrupt the main power path itself — it controls the contactor coil.

  1. Sensing connections (L1, L2, L3 terminals on relay): Connect to the three phases on the supply side (line side) of the main contactor. These sense the incoming supply voltage and sequence.
  2. Power supply for relay electronics: On most DIN-rail relays, the electronics are powered from two of the same three sensing terminals (typically L1 and L3). No separate supply needed.
  3. Output contact (NC — normally closed): Wire the normally closed (NC) output contact of the relay in series with the control circuit of the main contactor. When a fault occurs, the NC contact opens, dropping out the contactor, which removes power from the motor.
  4. Manual reset / auto-reset selection: Most adjustable relays offer both. For WAPDA conditions, set to auto-restart with a 30–60 second delay — this prevents the motor restarting on a momentary phase dip and confirms the supply is stable before reconnecting.
  5. Commissioning test: With the motor running, temporarily disconnect one phase to verify the relay trips within the set delay time (typically 0.1–3 seconds for phase loss). Reconnect and confirm auto-restart (if enabled) after the set delay.

Wiring note for Pakistan: Always mount the relay in an IP54 or better enclosure. Pakistan's dusty environment (especially agricultural areas) causes insulation tracking across exposed relay terminals within 2–3 years. A well-sealed DB or MCC panel extends relay life from 2 years to 10+ years.

The overload relay is wired in the main current path (series with the motor). The phase monitoring relay is wired in the control circuit only. They work independently and in combination: if the motor overloads mechanically, the overload relay trips; if WAPDA fails a phase, the monitoring relay trips first. Every complete motor starter in Pakistan should include both.

Phase Reversal: The Hidden Risk After WAPDA Maintenance

Phase reversal (wrong phase sequence) is one of the most under-appreciated failure modes in Pakistani motor installations. Symptoms of reversed phase sequence:

  • Centrifugal pump delivers no water (impeller spinning backwards)
  • Compressor runs but produces no pressure (scroll or piston compressor runs backwards)
  • Lift / conveyor belt moves in wrong direction
  • Three-phase induction motor appears to start normally but load output drops to near-zero

Phase reversal is detected instantly (≤0.1 seconds) by any relay with phase sequence monitoring. The relay trips before the reversed motor can damage the connected load. Without phase sequence protection, a reversed motor can run for hours or days drawing full current with near-zero useful output — overheating and burning despite the overload relay not tripping (because the current is normal — just not productive).

Complete 3-Phase Motor Starter with Phase Protection — Component List

A complete DOL (Direct On Line) motor starter with phase protection for a Pakistani WAPDA-connected motor requires the following components:

  1. Main switch / MCCB — isolates the motor circuit for maintenance (sized at 1.0–1.25× motor FLA)
  2. AC magnetic contactor — switching device controlled by the control circuit (sized by motor HP/kW)
  3. Thermal or electronic overload relay — current-based protection, wired in the main path after the contactor
  4. Phase monitoring relay — voltage-based protection, wired in the control circuit, tripping the contactor coil
  5. Control transformer (optional) — steps 400V 3-phase down to 230V or 110V single-phase for the control circuit
  6. Start / Stop pushbuttons — NO (start) and NC (stop) momentary buttons for manual operation
  7. Indicator lamps — run (green), trip (red), power on (white)

The cable lugs for motor terminal connections must be correctly sized — motor terminals and contactor output terminals are the highest-heat joints in the starter assembly, and undersized or poorly crimped lugs are a common fire risk.

Frequently Asked Questions — Phase Sequence Relay Pakistan

What is the price of a phase sequence relay in Pakistan?

A basic phase sequence relay in Pakistan costs Rs 800–1,500. Multi-function phase monitoring relays (with under/over voltage and asymmetry protection) cost Rs 2,500–7,000. Digital DIN-rail motor protection relays cost Rs 8,000–35,000.

What is the difference between a phase sequence relay and a phase monitoring relay?

A basic phase sequence relay detects only wrong phase order (R-Y-B vs R-B-Y). A phase monitoring relay is more comprehensive — it also monitors phase loss (open phase), under-voltage, over-voltage, and phase asymmetry. For Pakistani installations, a phase monitoring relay is strongly recommended because WAPDA failures include all these fault types.

Why do motors burn in Pakistan and how does a phase monitoring relay help?

The three leading causes of 3-phase motor burnout in Pakistan are: (1) phase failure — one line goes dead but the motor keeps running on two phases at 173% rated current; (2) phase reversal — motor runs in the wrong direction, damaging pumps and compressors; (3) low voltage — WAPDA voltage dips cause motors to draw excess current and overheat. A phase monitoring relay disconnects the motor within 0.1–0.5 seconds of any of these events, preventing burnout.

How do I wire a phase sequence relay?

Wire the PSR sensing terminals to all three phases (L1, L2, L3) via the supply side of the main contactor. Connect the PSR output (NC contact) in series with the control coil circuit of the main contactor. When a fault occurs, the relay drops out the contactor, cutting power to the motor.

Can a phase sequence relay protect against single-phasing?

A basic phase sequence relay only detects wrong phase order, NOT single-phasing (phase loss). To protect against single-phasing, you need a phase monitoring relay or a phase failure relay. Look for relays labelled "phase loss protection" or "open phase protection" in the specifications.

What setting should I use for under-voltage protection in Pakistan?

WAPDA nominal is 400V line-to-line. Set under-voltage trip at 330–350V (82–87% of nominal). For rural agricultural feeders where low voltage is chronic, 320V is a practical lower limit. Setting too tight (e.g., 370V trip) will cause constant nuisance trips on Pakistani rural feeders during summer peak hours.
