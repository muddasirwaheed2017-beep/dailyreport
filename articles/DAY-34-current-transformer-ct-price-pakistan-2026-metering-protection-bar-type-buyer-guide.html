What Is a Current Transformer (CT) and How Does It Work?

A current transformer is an instrument transformer that produces a reduced, proportional replica of a large primary current on its secondary terminals — safe for measurement and protection instruments. The primary circuit (the cable carrying hundreds of amperes) passes through the CT core aperture. The magnetic flux induces a proportional secondary current, which is fed to an energy meter, power analyzer, or protective relay.

Example: a 400A/5A CT with 400 amperes flowing in the primary produces exactly 5 amperes on the secondary. An energy meter reads the 5A secondary and multiplies internally by the CT ratio (80) to display the actual primary current. This lets a small, standard 5A meter measure a 400A bus without direct connection — critical for safety and for standardizing metering equipment.

Key parameters every buyer must know:

  • Primary rating (In): The maximum continuous current the CT is designed to measure (e.g., 200A, 400A, 1000A).
  • Secondary rating: Almost always 5A in Pakistan (some imports use 1A — check before ordering).
  • Accuracy class: How precise the ratio is at operating current levels (0.5S, 1.0, 5P20, etc.).
  • Burden (VA): The maximum load the CT can drive on its secondary without losing accuracy (2.5VA, 5VA, 10VA, 15VA).
  • Accuracy Limit Factor (ALF): Applies to protection CTs — the multiple of rated current up to which the CT stays accurate (5P10 = accurate to 10× In; 5P20 = accurate to 20× In).
  • Core aperture: The hole diameter or window size — must fit around your cable or busbar.

Important safety rule: NEVER open-circuit the secondary of a CT while primary current is flowing. With the secondary open, all the primary flux produces a dangerously high voltage on the secondary terminals (potentially thousands of volts). Always short-circuit the secondary terminals before disconnecting any instrument, and only open after primary current is de-energized.

Why CTs Matter in Pakistan — WAPDA Compliance and Revenue Metering

Pakistan's national utility (WAPDA / DISCOs) uses CT-operated metering for all industrial and large commercial connections above approximately 100kVA. The CT ratio determines how the energy meter calculates consumption — a wrong CT ratio or inaccurate CT means systematic billing errors, either overpaying or underpaying for electricity. WAPDA meter inspectors can issue penalties for non-compliant metering CTs.

WAPDA metering CT requirements (Pakistan LT industrial connections):

  • Accuracy class: 0.5S minimum (not 1.0, not 3.0, not protection class)
  • Burden: Match CT burden to actual secondary load (cable + meter burden must be ≤ CT rated burden)
  • CT ratio: Must match the ratio programmed in the approved energy meter
  • Terminal blocks: CT secondary must terminate at a dedicated CT shorting terminal block before the meter — enables meter replacement without open-circuiting the CT
  • Sealing: WAPDA seals the CT terminal covers and meter — only WAPDA staff may open them on approved metering CTs

For 3-phase energy meter installations in Pakistan, matching CT ratio to the meter's programmed ratio is the most common installation error — resulting in readings that are 2× or 0.5× actual consumption. Always confirm the meter's CT ratio setting before panel commissioning.

CT Types Available in Pakistan

1. Wound-Type Metering CT

The most common type in the 50A–600A range. The primary conductor makes one or more turns through the CT core — built into the CT housing. Compact, accurate, cost-effective. Used in distribution boards, commercial meter panels, and LT industrial panels. Available in DIN rail mount or panel-mount formats. Price: PKR 350–2,200.

2. Bar-Type CT (Through-Core CT)

The primary winding is a single straight bus bar or cable that passes through the CT aperture — the CT itself has no primary winding. For ratings above 600A, bar-type CTs are the only practical option: the busbar is the primary. Available with rectangular or circular apertures. The CT mounts on the busbar; secondary leads connect to the metering or protection panel. Price: PKR 2,000–6,500 for 800A–2000A ratings.

3. Protection CT

Designed to remain linear at fault currents (5P10: up to 10× rated; 5P20: up to 20× rated). Connected to earth leakage relays, overcurrent relays, and motor protection units. Protection CTs deliberately saturate at high currents to protect connected instruments — metering CTs do the same, but their saturation point is calibrated to occur before damage to the energy meter. Price: PKR 700–3,500.

4. Split-Core CT (Clip-On / Rogowski)

The core opens on a hinge — clip around an existing live cable without disconnecting it. Best for energy audits, retrofit monitoring, and sub-metering installations. Accuracy class 1.0 or 3.0 (not 0.5S). Rogowski coil variant is flexible and can wrap around any conductor shape. Price: PKR 1,200–4,500.

5. Zero-Sequence CT (Core Balance CT / CBCT)

A large-aperture CT where all three phase conductors pass through a single core simultaneously. Under normal balanced conditions, the vector sum of three-phase currents is zero — the CT output is zero. During an earth fault, residual current creates a CT output that trips the earth fault relay. Used in earth leakage protection for industrial panels. Aperture must accommodate all three phase cables — order based on cable diameter, not current rating. Price: PKR 800–3,500 depending on aperture size.

CT Burden Calculation — Pakistan Panel Builder's Guide

Burden is the total VA consumed by the CT secondary circuit. Exceeding the CT's rated burden causes the CT to saturate, giving inaccurate readings. Calculate burden before ordering to avoid the most expensive metering mistake in Pakistani panel building.

Burden sources in a typical Pakistan panel:

  1. Energy meter secondary burden: Shown on meter nameplate — typically 0.5VA–2.0VA per phase for electronic energy meters.
  2. Power factor relay/monitor: Typically 2.0VA–5.0VA per phase (check datasheet).
  3. Ammeter (if fitted): Moving iron ammeters draw 3VA–10VA — major burden! Electronic ammeters draw <1VA.
  4. CT terminal block and wiring resistance: Secondary cable resistance × secondary current² = burden in VA. Use 2.5mm² copper cable; calculate both outgoing and return conductor lengths.

CT in Panel Wiring — Pakistan Engineer's Reference

A complete LT industrial metering and protection panel in Pakistan typically requires:

  1. Main incomer ACB/MCCB — with built-in amperometer or separate CT for ammeter
  2. 3× metering CTs (0.5S) — one per phase on incomer cable/busbar
  3. 3× protection CTs (5P20) — one per phase feeding overcurrent relay
  4. 1× CBCT (core balance CT) — for earth leakage relay
  5. CT shorting terminal block — allows meter replacement without open-circuiting CTs
  6. 3-phase energy meter — connected via metering CT secondary
  7. Power factor relay — connected to metering CT secondary (after energy meter)
  8. Overcurrent relay (IDMTL) — connected to protection CT secondary
  9. Earth fault relay — connected to CBCT secondary
  10. Secondary CT wiring — 2.5mm² copper, dedicated single-pair from each CT to terminal block

For motor starter panels, protection CTs feed the thermal overload relay or motor protection relay. The phase sequence relay monitors voltage — it does not require CT input, only VT (voltage transformer) or direct voltage connection.

Common CT Installation Mistakes in Pakistan

  1. Mismatched CT ratio and energy meter programming: Buying a 400A/5A CT but leaving the meter programmed for 100A/5A — energy readings are 4× actual consumption (or 0.25× depending on direction of mismatch). Always confirm meter CT ratio setting on commissioning.
  2. Open-circuiting the CT secondary: Removing the energy meter (for replacement or testing) without first shorting the CT secondary via the shorting terminal block. Risk: high voltage across open secondary, damage to CT, injury to personnel.
  3. Using 1.5mm² CT wiring on long runs: Adds 6–12VA of burden on a 5VA-rated CT, putting the CT in saturation during normal operation. All readings will be systematically low.
  4. Installing a metering CT for relay protection: Class 0.5S CTs saturate at 120% of rated current by design — they will not pass fault current to overcurrent relays accurately. Motor trips will be delayed or fail entirely.
  5. Wrong CT polarity: CT secondary terminals are marked P1/P2 (primary) and S1/S2 (secondary). Reversing S1/S2 doesn't affect kWh total readings but reverses power factor indication and reactive power sign — causes errors in power factor correction panels.
  6. Buying a 1A secondary CT for a 5A input meter: CTs come in 5A and 1A secondary ratings. Pakistan distribution panels almost universally use 5A secondary. Connecting a 1A CT to a 5A meter input gives readings at 1/5 of actual. Check the nameplate secondary rating before purchase.

Frequently Asked Questions — Current Transformers Pakistan 2026

What is the price of a current transformer in Pakistan in 2026?

Current transformer prices in Pakistan in 2026 range from PKR 350–800 for a standard 100A/5A metering CT, PKR 800–2,500 for a 200A–600A metering CT with class 0.5S accuracy, and PKR 1,500–6,000 for a 1000A–2000A bar-type CT. Protection CTs (5P10/5P20) in the 100A–600A range cost PKR 700–3,500 depending on ratio and burden class. Split-core (clip-on) CTs for retrofit metering cost PKR 1,200–4,000.

What accuracy class CT is required for WAPDA revenue metering in Pakistan?

WAPDA requires accuracy class 0.5S for revenue metering CTs on industrial and commercial connections. Class 0.5S maintains accuracy from 1% to 120% of rated current — critical for accurate billing at low-load conditions. Never use a protection CT (class 5P or 10P) for revenue metering.

What is CT burden and why does it matter in Pakistan?

CT burden is the total load impedance connected to the CT secondary. Exceeding the rated burden causes CT saturation and inaccurate readings. In Pakistan, long cable runs from CT to meter are common — use 2.5mm² copper cable and keep total secondary cable run under 15 metres for a 5VA CT, or upgrade to a 10VA CT for longer runs.

What is the difference between metering CT and protection CT?

Metering CTs (class 0.5S, 1.0) are accurate at normal currents but saturate during faults — protecting the energy meter. Protection CTs (class 5P10, 5P20) stay linear at high fault currents so protective relays operate correctly. Never cross-use: a protection CT on a revenue meter gives inaccurate billing; a metering CT feeding a relay may fail to trip during faults.

Can I use a split-core CT without disconnecting cables?

Yes — split-core CTs clip around existing cables without disconnection. Ideal for retrofit energy monitoring. However, they are typically class 1.0 or 3.0 — not suitable for WAPDA revenue metering (requires 0.5S). Best for power monitoring and energy auditing on live panels.

What CT ratio should I use for my electrical panel in Pakistan?

Select a ratio where your maximum expected load is between 60% and 100% of the CT primary rating. For a 200A incomer with 160A maximum load, use 200A/5A. For WAPDA metering, the CT ratio must match the energy meter's programmed ratio — always confirm before commissioning.
