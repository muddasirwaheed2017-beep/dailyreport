What Is a Busbar? How It Works in a Switchboard

A busbar is a rigid conductor — typically flat copper or aluminium bar — that distributes electrical power from an incoming supply to multiple outgoing circuits inside a switchboard, distribution board, motor control centre (MCC), or industrial panel. Unlike flexible cables, a busbar can carry very high currents (100A to 6,300A) in a compact, well-supported, and thermally efficient form.

In a typical Pakistani LT panel, the incoming WAPDA supply (415V, 3-phase) connects to three phase busbars (R, Y, B) and one neutral busbar (N), all supported by insulated busbar supports mounted to the back plate. Circuit breakers, MCBs, and contactors then tap off these busbars via bolted connections. Earth (PE) busbars provide the earthing reference for the panel enclosure.

Key busbar parameters every panel builder must know:

  • Cross-section (mm²) — determines current-carrying capacity (ampacity)
  • Dimensions (width × thickness in mm) — standard flat bar format: 25×3, 40×5, 50×5, 63×6, 80×8, 100×10, 120×10
  • Material — electrolytic tough pitch (ETP) copper or 1350-H19 aluminium alloy
  • Surface finish — bare, tin-plated, or silver-plated at contact points
  • Short-circuit withstand (kA) — how much fault current the busbar system can survive without damage

Why Busbars Are Critical in Pakistan — The Local Challenges

1. High Ambient Temperature Reduces Ampacity Severely

Standard busbar ampacity tables published by IEC 60439 assume 35°C ambient. Pakistani switchrooms, especially in summer, routinely reach 45–55°C. At 50°C ambient, a 50×5 mm copper busbar rated 430A at 35°C can only safely carry 340A (430A × 0.79 de-rating factor). Ignoring this is the single most common cause of busbar overheating and panel fires in Pakistan.

2. Coastal Humidity and Corrosion (Karachi)

Karachi's salt-laden humid air (relative humidity 70–90% in monsoon) aggressively oxidises bare aluminium busbar joints and un-plated copper surfaces. Aluminium oxide has very high contact resistance — a 10 mΩ joint resistance on a 400A circuit generates 1.6 kW of heat at the joint. In Karachi, always specify tin-plated contact surfaces on copper busbars, and use anodised aluminium with joint compound on aluminium busbars.

3. Load Shedding Thermal Cycling

Pakistan's load-shedding cycle (power on → off → on) subjects busbars to repeated thermal expansion and contraction. Poorly torqued bolted joints loosen over 50–100 cycles, increasing contact resistance, causing hot-spots, and eventually arcing. Use spring washers (Belleville washers) at all busbar joints and re-torque after the first week of operation.

4. WAPDA Metering Panel Busbar Clearance

WAPDA's metering panel specification (for industrial consumers above 50 kW) requires minimum air clearance between phase busbars of 25 mm at 415V. Many budget panel builders use busbar supports that are too close, risking flashover. Always use IEC 60439-compliant busbar supports with proper phase-to-phase and phase-to-earth clearance markings. Busbar in a Pakistan Panel — Component Reference

A standard 3-phase LT panel in Pakistan uses the following busbar components:

  1. Phase busbars (R, Y, B) — 3 × copper flat bars, sized for total panel load
  2. Neutral busbar (N) — same cross-section as phase for TN-S systems; half cross-section for TN-C-S
  3. Earth/PE busbar — green-yellow marked, minimum 50% of phase cross-section (IEC 60439)
  4. Busbar supports (insulators) — DMC/SMC insulator blocks, 25 mm min. phase-to-phase clearance
  5. Busbar end caps — insulated, colour-coded (red/yellow/blue/black for R/Y/B/N)
  6. Busbar joints — overlapping bolted connections with spring (Belleville) washers
  7. Busbar shrouding — insulating covers over live busbars per IEC 61439 IP2X requirement
  8. Busbar risers/droppers — vertical connections from main busbar to breaker terminals
  9. CT mounting (for metering) — busbars pass through the CT window; see CT guide for sizing
  10. Incoming isolator — main switch or MCB/MCCB connected to incomer side of busbar
  11. SPD connection — surge protection device tapped from busbar with short leads (<0.5 m) for best performance
  12. Energy meter CTs — connected to busbars for kWh metering; see 3-phase energy meter guide

Pakistan Panel Builder's Busbar Installation Checklist

Pre-Installation

  • ☐ Calculate FLC for transformer/load, add 25% margin, apply temperature de-rating (use 45–50°C for Pakistan)
  • ☐ Select busbar cross-section from de-rated ampacity table above
  • ☐ Verify short-circuit withstand (kA) rating of busbar supports and the busbar itself
  • ☐ Confirm phase-to-phase clearance: minimum 25 mm (415V), 36 mm (6.6 kV panels)
  • ☐ For Karachi coastal: specify tin-plated copper busbars and corrosion-resistant hardware

Installation

  • ☐ Support busbars at maximum 600 mm intervals (IEC 61439)
  • ☐ Colour-code: Red (L1/R), Yellow (L2/Y), Blue (L3/B), Black/Grey (N), Green-Yellow (PE)
  • ☐ Torque all bolted joints: M8=20 Nm, M10=40 Nm, M12=70 Nm — use calibrated torque wrench
  • ☐ Apply joint compound (NO-OX-ID or equivalent) on aluminium busbar contact surfaces
  • ☐ Install spring (Belleville) washers under all joint bolts
  • ☐ Mark all torqued bolts with witness paint or marker line
  • ☐ Install insulating shrouds/covers over all exposed live busbars

Post-Installation / Commissioning

  • ☐ Verify insulation resistance busbar-to-earth: minimum 1 MΩ at 500V DC per phase
  • ☐ Check phase sequence with phase sequence indicator before closing breakers
  • ☐ Record IR values in commissioning record for future comparison
  • ☐ After 48 hours under load: re-torque all busbar joints (thermal cycling loosens connections)
  • ☐ Annual IR testing and visual thermographic inspection recommended

Common Busbar Mistakes in Pakistan — 6 Expensive Errors

Mistake 1: Not De-Rating for Pakistan's Ambient Temperature

Selecting busbar size from IEC tables assuming 35°C ambient, then installing in a 50°C switchroom. A 50×5 mm copper busbar on a 400A load is fine at 35°C (430A rated) but overloaded at 50°C (only 340A de-rated). Result: overheating, insulation failure, fire. Fix: always size for the worst-case summer ambient in the actual installation location.

Mistake 2: Under-Torquing Busbar Joints

Hand-tightening M10 bolts (~8 Nm) instead of the required 40 Nm. Under-torqued joints have 5–10× higher contact resistance, generating heat at every joint. After 20–30 load-shedding cycles, the joint loosens further, eventually causing arc-flash. Fix: always use a calibrated torque wrench and re-torque after the first week.

Mistake 3: Using Aluminium Busbars Without Joint Compound in Karachi

Bare aluminium oxidises in humid air within days. Aluminium oxide at a busbar joint has resistance 1,000× higher than the metal. Visible sign: overheating and charring at joints within 6–12 months. Fix: apply NO-OX-ID or conductive joint compound at every aluminium contact surface before bolting. Repeat at annual maintenance.

Mistake 4: Ignoring Short-Circuit Withstand Rating

Specifying busbar size for normal current only, without checking kA withstand. When a fault occurs on the WAPDA system (fault levels at industrial feeders typically 25–35 kA in major Pakistani cities), undersized busbars can be destroyed or fused together, creating a fire. Fix: check the declared fault level from WAPDA and verify busbar kA rating matches or exceeds it.

Mistake 5: Wrong Phase Colour Coding

Using any available cable sleeving colour instead of the standard R/Y/B (or Red/Yellow/Blue for phase, Black for neutral in Pakistani practice). Wrong colour coding causes dangerous confusion during live maintenance. Fix: use IEC 60445-compliant colour-coded heat-shrink sleeves or busbar end caps at every panel — non-negotiable for safety.

Mistake 6: Insufficient Busbar Support Spacing

Mounting busbar supports more than 600 mm apart to save cost. Under fault current, the magnetic force between busbars tries to push them apart (parallel currents attract/repel). Widely spaced busbars flex, potentially cracking the insulator supports and causing phase-to-phase contact. Fix: maximum 600 mm support spacing per IEC 61439; reduce to 400 mm for busbars above 1,000A.

Frequently Asked Questions — Busbar Pakistan 2026

What is the price of copper busbar in Pakistan in 2026?

Copper busbar in Pakistan 2026 ranges from Rs 180–400 per metre for standard sizes (25×3 mm to 50×5 mm), and Rs 600–1,800 per metre for heavy industrial sizes (80×10 mm to 120×10 mm). Prices vary by purity grade (ETP copper vs electrolytic), tin-plating, and supplier. Karachi and Lahore have the widest availability.

How do I de-rate busbar ampacity for Pakistan's high ambient temperature?

Standard busbar ampacity tables are based on 35°C ambient. In Pakistan, ambient temperatures in electrical rooms regularly reach 40–55°C. Apply de-rating factors: at 40°C apply 0.94×, at 45°C apply 0.87×, at 50°C apply 0.79×, at 55°C apply 0.71×. Example: a 50×5 mm copper busbar rated 430A at 35°C carries only 340A (430×0.79) in a 50°C switchroom — always size for worst-case summer ambient.

Copper busbar vs aluminium busbar — which is better for Pakistan?

Copper busbar is preferred for LT panels, distribution boards, and motor control centres in Pakistan because it has 60% higher conductivity than aluminium, handles higher fault current, and resists oxidation in Pakistan's humid coastal environments. Aluminium busbar is cost-effective for large transmission spans (>10 metres) where weight matters, but requires tin-plated contact surfaces to prevent oxide build-up at joints. For Karachi coastal installations, always specify tin-plated copper or anodised aluminium.

What size busbar do I need for a 100 kVA, 200 kVA, or 400 kVA transformer in Pakistan?

100 kVA 3-phase (415V): full-load current 139A → minimum 25×5 mm copper busbar (de-rated at 45°C). 200 kVA: FLC 278A → minimum 40×5 mm copper (check margin). 400 kVA: FLC 556A → minimum 80×8 mm copper (with 25% margin and 45°C de-rating). Always add 25% safety margin and apply temperature de-rating. For 630 kVA and above, consider sandwich busbar trunking.

What is busbar trunking and when should I use it in Pakistan?

Busbar trunking (BBT) is an enclosed, prefabricated power distribution system used where large currents (400A–6,300A) must be distributed over long distances safely. In Pakistan, BBT is used in factories, hospitals, data centres, and high-rise buildings where traditional cable runs would be bulky and hazardous. Sandwich-type BBT with IP54 or IP65 rating is recommended for Karachi coastal and industrial environments. Cost is Rs 4,500–45,000 per metre depending on current rating.

What is the correct torque for busbar bolted joints in a Pakistan distribution panel?

Busbar joint bolt torque depends on bolt size: M8 bolt = 20 Nm, M10 bolt = 40 Nm, M12 bolt = 70 Nm. Under-torqued joints cause high contact resistance, hot-spots, and fire risk — a common cause of panel failures in Pakistan. Always use spring washers, apply joint compound (NO-OX-ID or equivalent) on aluminium busbars, re-torque after first thermal cycle (48 hours under load). Mark torqued bolts with a witness mark.
