2. MCB — the workhorse

The MCB is what fills the slot in 95% of Pakistani DB-boxes. It protects against:

  • Overload — slow trip (1-5 minutes) when current exceeds rating by 1.13-1.45×
  • Short-circuit — fast trip (10-100 milliseconds) when current exceeds rating by 3-10× (depending on B, C, or D curve)

Best for: All residential circuits up to 63A. Lights, sockets, fans, ACs, geyser, tubewell motors up to 5 HP.

3. MCCB — for higher current + adjustable trip

MCCB is essentially a larger MCB with:

  • Higher amperage rating (63A-1600A)
  • Higher breaking capacity (36 kA+ vs 6-10 kA for MCB)
  • Adjustable trip settings (you can fine-tune the trip current)
  • Larger physical size (panel-mounted, not DIN-rail)

Best for: Main DB-box incoming feed, light commercial main, sub-main feeds for industrial panels, larger motors (10-100 HP).

See our MCCB + ACB industrial guide.

4. RCCB — earth-leakage protection ONLY

An RCCB is NOT a circuit breaker in the overcurrent sense. It detects:

  • Earth leakage — current going to earth (typically via a person touching live equipment)
  • Trips when leakage exceeds sensitivity (30 mA for residential shock protection, 100-300 mA for industrial fire protection)

Important: An RCCB alone provides NO overload or short-circuit protection. Always pair with MCB upstream.

Best for: Whole-house DB-box feed, with MCBs downstream for individual circuit overload protection.

5. RCBO — MCB + RCCB in one DIN slot

An RCBO combines the functions of MCB + RCCB in a single device occupying 1 DIN slot. It gives you:

  • Overload protection (MCB function)
  • Short-circuit protection (MCB function)
  • Earth-leakage protection (RCCB function)

Best for: Per-circuit installation where selectivity matters. One RCBO per circuit (geyser, AC, kitchen, etc.) so a fault on one circuit doesn't kill the whole house.

6. Pakistani DB-box configuration patterns

Pattern 1 — Budget (cheapest)

  • Main MCB at the incoming feed
  • One MCB per circuit
  • No earth-leakage protection (illegal under PEC code but common in older homes)

Pattern 2 — Standard residential (PEC compliant)

  • Main MCB at incoming feed
  • One RCCB 30 mA covering all socket circuits
  • One MCB per circuit downstream of the RCCB

Cost for 8 circuits: PKR ~5,000 for MCBs + PKR 3,500 RCCB = PKR 8,500.

Pattern 3 — Premium residential (selectivity)

  • Main MCB
  • One RCBO 30 mA per circuit (replacing MCB + shared RCCB)

Cost for 8 circuits: 8 × PKR 4,200 = PKR 33,600. Higher but each circuit isolates independently on fault.

Pattern 4 — Solar prosumer (NEPRA 2026)

  • Pattern 3 plus dedicated Type B RCBO on solar feed (Type AC won't pass inspection)

8. FAQ

MCB vs RCBO — which to use?

MCB protects against overcurrent. RCBO adds earth-leakage protection. PEC code requires earth-leakage protection on socket circuits, so RCBO (or MCB+RCCB combo) is mandatory for those. For lighting-only circuits, MCB alone is technically acceptable.

Cheapest combination for a 3-bed Pakistani home?

Main MCB + 1 shared RCCB + 8 MCBs = PKR 8,500. Pattern 2 above.

What's the difference between MCB and MCCB?

MCB is for 6-125A residential. MCCB is for 63-1600A commercial/industrial with adjustable trip. Same function, different scale.

Do I need Type B RCBO for solar?

Under NEPRA 2026 Prosumer Regulations, yes. Type AC fails DISCO inspection on the solar feed.

What does the kA rating mean?

kA = breaking capacity = how much fault current the breaker can interrupt without exploding. Residential MCBs are 6 kA (fine for Pakistani distribution). Commercial MCCBs are 25-36 kA. Industrial are 50-65 kA.
