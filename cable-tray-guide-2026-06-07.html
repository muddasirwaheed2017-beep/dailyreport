What Is a Cable Tray?

A cable tray is a rigid structural system — made from galvanized iron (GI), PVC, stainless steel, or aluminium — used to support insulated electrical cables and data conduits along the walls, ceilings, or floors of a building. It replaces loose conduit runs and gives installers a single, open-access route that can be re-cabled without demolition.

Cable trays are mandatory by NEPRA/NEC code in industrial and commercial electrical installations wherever cable runs exceed 3 metres in length. They are used in:

  • Industrial factories and process plants
  • Commercial buildings and data centres
  • Solar power plants and switchrooms
  • Hospitals and educational institutions
  • Outdoor substations and utility corridors

4 Main Types of Cable Tray — Which One Do You Need?

1. Perforated Cable Tray

The most common type in Pakistan. A flat-bottomed tray with punched holes for ventilation. The holes allow heat to escape from high-current cables while the solid sides protect against accidental contact. Best for: general power and control cable runs in factories, office buildings, and solar switchrooms.

  • Width range: 50 mm to 600 mm
  • Standard depth: 50 mm, 75 mm, 100 mm
  • Material: GI HDG (hot-dip galvanized) recommended for outdoor use

2. Ladder Cable Tray

Two parallel side rails connected by rungs (like a ladder). Maximum ventilation, lowest weight, and best suited for large power cables (70 mm² and above) and cable bundles. Best for: power plants, petrochemical facilities, heavy industry.

  • Rung spacing: 150 mm, 250 mm, 300 mm
  • Handles the heaviest cable loads
  • Available in hot-dip galvanized or epoxy-coated finish

3. Wire Mesh / Cable Basket

Made from welded wire mesh, this flexible tray can be cut and shaped without special tools. Ideal for data centres, server rooms, and low-voltage control cables. Best for: Cat 6 / fibre optic runs, CCTV and alarm systems.

  • Very easy to install and re-route
  • Lightweight — saves ceiling load
  • Not recommended for >1 kV power cables

4. Solid Bottom / Enclosed Tray

No ventilation holes — provides maximum protection against dripping water and falling debris. Required for instrumentation cables, coaxial and signal cables that must be shielded from electromagnetic interference.

  • Typically paired with a fitted cover
  • Used in chemical plants and food processing
  • Available with optional cable covers for IP54 ingress protection

How to Size a Cable Tray — Step-by-Step

  1. List all cables: Note each cable's outer diameter (OD) from its datasheet.
  2. Calculate fill area: Sum the cross-sectional areas (π × r²) of all cables in the run.
  3. Apply the 40% fill rule: Per NEC 392/NEPRA guidelines, cables should occupy no more than 40% of usable tray cross-section. So: Tray Width × Tray Depth × 0.40 ≥ total cable fill area.
  4. Add 25% spare capacity for future cable additions.
  5. Check span tables: Ensure the tray span between supports does not exceed manufacturer's deflection limits (typically 1,500 mm for standard GI).

Quick Rule of Thumb:

  • Up to 10 cables (≤16 mm²): 100 mm wide tray
  • 10–20 cables (16–35 mm²): 200 mm wide tray
  • 20–40 cables or large 70–150 mm² cables: 300–400 mm wide tray
  • Major power feeder runs: 400–600 mm wide ladder tray

Cable Tray Installation Tips for Pakistan

  • Grounding: Every GI tray section must be bonded and earthed. Use bare copper 4 mm² bonding wire between sections with two-hole lug connectors.
  • Support spacing: Maximum 1,500 mm between wall brackets for standard GI perforated tray. Reduce to 1,200 mm for heavy ladder tray with large cables.
  • Segregation: Never run high-voltage power cables and signal/data cables in the same tray without a metal divider plate. Use separate trays with 300 mm separation where possible.
  • Bend radius: All cables must observe the minimum bend radius (typically 6–12× cable OD) at tray bends and tee-junctions.
  • Outdoor sealing: Use UV-resistant PVC conduit seals at entry/exit points in outdoor trays to prevent water ingress.
  • Fire stops: Seal cable tray penetrations through fire-rated walls with approved fire-stop putty or pillows.

Q2: What is the difference between a cable tray and a cable conduit?

A cable conduit is a closed pipe through which cables are pulled — not accessible without cutting. A cable tray is an open or semi-open support system where cables lie in full view and can be added, removed, or rerouted without tools. Cable trays are preferred for large installations due to better heat dissipation, lower installation cost, and easier maintenance.

Q3: What is GI cable tray and why is it preferred?

GI (Galvanized Iron) cable tray is made from mild steel that is zinc-coated to prevent rust. Hot-dip galvanized (HDG) trays offer the best protection — the zinc coating is 85–100 µm thick, far superior to the 8–15 µm of pre-galvanized (electro-zinc) trays. In Pakistan's humid and coastal conditions, HDG GI cable tray is the standard for all outdoor and industrial applications.

Q4: What size cable tray do I need for my project?

Use the 40% fill rule: the sum of cable cross-sectional areas should not exceed 40% of tray cross-section. As a quick guide: for up to 10 × 4 mm² cables, a 100 mm wide tray is adequate; for 20+ heavy cables (50–120 mm²), use 400–600 mm wide ladder tray.
